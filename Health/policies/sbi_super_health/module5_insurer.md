# Module 5 — Insurer Quality

_Source: **ICRA rating rationale, 1-Dec-2025** (authoritative — read by parsing the PDF, not scraping) + IRDAI warnings & penalties + FY24-25 IRDAI claims data (via M3). Weight: **10%**._
_Profile studied: **Individual (single adult), age 26, metro tier-1** — a **40-year** hold._

> **Plain-English intro.** This module asks one question: **"Will this company still exist, still be solvent, and still treat me fairly in 2066?"** Three terms do the work:
> - **Solvency ratio** = spare capital vs what regulators require. **1.50 is the legal floor.** Higher = safer.
> - **Combined ratio** = of every ₹100 of premium, how much goes out as claims **+ costs**. **Over 100% = the insurer loses money on insurance itself**, living off investment returns or parent capital.
> - **Credit rating (AAA)** = an independent agency's verdict on ability to pay. AAA is the top of the scale.
>
> **The tension here:** SBI General has **the strongest parent in the study** — and **loses money underwriting**. Both are true; this module resolves how much that should worry you.

## 🚩 The central question: does a loss-making insurer threaten *this* policyholder?

The framework's **HDFC M5 dimension** warns: *"a chronically loss-making insurer tends to tighten claims scrutiny or push through steeper premium hikes."* SBI General **is** chronically loss-making. So how worried should a 26-year-old be?

```
   Combined ratio = (claims + costs) per ₹100 of premium
   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   100% = break-even on insurance
                                                    
   SBI General blended, FY22-25 avg  ▓▓▓▓▓ 109.6%   ← loses ₹9.6 per ₹100
     └─ Motor              FY2024    ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ 126.4%
     └─ Motor              FY2025    ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ 134.8%  ⚠️ worsening — the drag
     └─ PA / Home (via SBI)          ✅ profitable
     └─ Retail health                ❓ unverified (but ICR/CSR show no distress)
   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   H1 FY2026: underwriting still weak, BUT annualised RoE 17.3% ✅
```

| The worry | The evidence | Verdict |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| *"They'll tighten claims to stem losses"* | Health **ICR 87.86%** (rising, above industry), **CSR 96.13%**, complaints **down 38%** — all recorded **during** the loss-making years | ❌ **Not happening.** Generosity is real and concurrent with the losses |
| *"They'll hike my premium"* | **The live risk.** A 109.6% combined ratio is precisely the pressure that drives the **§G.n block re-pricing** M4 could not quantify (revision history unverified). **M5 supplies the motive M4 lacked** | ⚠️ **Real, unquantified** — the strongest support for M4's repricing caveat |
| *"They might not survive"* | **SBI 73.9% + [ICRA]AAA + ₹689 Cr equity + ₹700 Cr sub-debt + ₹510 Cr headroom + CEO on deputation from SBI** | ❌ **Negligible.** About as certain as Indian insurance allows |
| *"The losses are in MY line"* | The drag is **motor (134.8%)**; PA/home profitable; health ICR/CSR healthy | ✅ **Mostly no** — see new dimension #1 |

> **A causal irony (inference, not established fact):** SBI General's two IRDAI penalties were for **under-writing its mandatory Motor-TP quota** in FY2017-19. It subsequently expanded motor to **32% of GDPI** — and motor is now the segment dragging its combined ratio to **134.8%**. **The regulator pushed it into the line that now loses it money.** ICRA does not draw this link; marked as observation.

## 🆕 NEW DIMENSIONS discovered in this module *(Rule 3)*

### 1. Attribute the combined ratio BY SEGMENT — whose losses are they? *(→ new bullet in study_plan M5)*
The framework treats a loss-making insurer as a uniform red flag. **Too blunt for a multi-line insurer.** SBI General's 109.6% blended ratio is **driven by motor (134.8%)**, while **PA and home are profitable** and the **health book shows an 87.86% ICR, 96% CSR and falling complaints** — no distress at all in the line this buyer is purchasing.
> **New check:** *"Attribute the combined ratio by segment. Is the loss in the line you're buying, or elsewhere? A motor-driven loss threatens a health policyholder only **indirectly** — via group-level repricing pressure and capital consumption — not directly through claims handling. A loss concentrated **in health** is a direct warning; a blended ratio alone cannot tell them apart."*

### 2. Retail-health share of the book — core customer or rounding error? *(→ new bullet in study_plan M5)*
The framework has a *"SAHI vs general insurer servicing focus"* bullet but **never quantifies it**. Computed here: **Health & PA = 35.2% of GDPI**, but **group health = 64.3% of health GDPI** → **retail health ≈ 12.6% of the entire company**. This buyer is a small minority customer of a motor/crop/fire/group-health business.
> **New check:** *"Compute retail health as a % of the insurer's GDPI. It cuts both ways — a line that is a rounding error is more exposed to **repricing, deprioritisation and product withdrawal** (feeds M6) and attracts less product investment; but a multi-line parent can **cross-subsidise** health losses, which a pure-play SAHI (Care, Niva — retail health ≈ the whole company) cannot. Score the trade-off; don't assume either structure is better."*

### 3. Strip the sub-debt — core solvency, and the rating trigger is the real floor *(→ new bullet in study_plan M5; row above)*
The framework says *"Solvency ratio (min 1.5)"*. Two corrections: **(a)** SBI's headline **2.13× includes ₹700 Cr of sub-debt — borrowed, not equity. Core solvency ex-sub-debt is 1.84×.** **(b)** IRDAI's 1.50 is **not the operative floor** — **ICRA's negative trigger is 1.70×**, and breaching it means a **downgrade**, which is what actually forces management action. On core solvency SBI sits **0.14 above its rating trigger**, not 0.63 above the regulatory one.
> **New check:** *"Always obtain **core solvency excluding sub-debt**, and benchmark it against the **rating agency's negative trigger**, not IRDAI's 1.50 floor. The headline can be debt-flattered, and the binding constraint is the rating, not the regulation."*

**Module 5 score (1–5): 4.0 / 5**

**Rationale.** On the question this module actually asks — **"will they survive and stay fair for 40 years?"** — SBI General gives close to the strongest answer available in Indian insurance. It is **[ICRA]AAA (Stable), reaffirmed 1-Dec-2025**, **73.9%-owned by State Bank of India, itself AAA**, with a linkage that is structural rather than nominal (shared brand, SBI's Chairman chairing the insurer, the **MD & CEO on deputation from SBI**, 22,500 branches feeding distribution) and **demonstrated, repeated capital support** (₹689 Cr equity 2023, ₹700 Cr sub-debt 2024, ~₹510 Cr headroom remaining). It is the **6th-largest private general insurer**, 16 years old, and — decisively for the framework's **foreign-JV-exit test** — **IAG's 2020 departure is six years digested and the AAA was reaffirmed five years after it**, resting on the parent that stayed. **Multi-line diversification also allows health losses to be cross-subsidised**, which pure-play SAHI rivals (Care, Niva) cannot do. **Conduct is clean where it matters:** the only penalties (₹55 lakh, 2021) were **motor-quota shortfalls, not claims or policyholder conduct**, with nothing since.

It is held to 4.0 by one genuine weakness: **SBI General loses money underwriting — an average combined ratio of 109.6% and RoE of just 7.3% across FY2022–FY2025**, with **motor deteriorating from 126.4% to 134.8%**. Three findings stop this costing more: the loss is **concentrated in motor, not health** (PA and home are profitable, and the health book pays generously — 87.86% ICR, 96.13% CSR, complaints down 38% — showing **no claims tightening despite the losses**); ICRA reads it as the **expected cost of deliberate retail expansion**; and **H1 FY2026 profitability rebounded to 17.3% annualised RoE**. Two cautions carry forward: the **109.6% combined ratio supplies the concrete motive for the §G.n block re-pricing that M4 could not quantify** — the single most likely channel by which SBI's economics reach this buyer — and **core solvency, stripped of sub-debt, is 1.84× against ICRA's 1.70 negative trigger**, a thinner buffer than the 2.13 headline implies.
