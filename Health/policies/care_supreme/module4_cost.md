# Module 4 — Cost & Premium

_Source: **Care Supreme policy wording** (UIN **CHIHLIP27061V032627**, v03) — **Annexure VI Benefit/Premium Illustration (rendered as images, per the METHOD RULE)**, §5.10(iv) no-claim-loading, §5.13 instalments, §5.14 revision power; **prospectus §2 Discount Schedule** + §7 Pre-Policy Medical grid; published aggregator quotes. Files in `resources/`._
_Profile studied: **Individual (single adult), age 26, metro tier-1 (Zone 1 Delhi-NCR / Zone 2 Mumbai MMR)**_
_Studied across SI tiers: **₹10L / ₹25L / ₹50L / ₹1Cr**_

> **Plain-English intro.** This module asks: **"What does it cost me — this year, and for the next 40 years?"** The traps are not in the first premium; they're in how it *grows*:
> - **Age band** = insurers re-price you as you get older. **Stepping up every 5 years (Care) is gentler than every year.**
> - **Escalator** = a *contractually printed* annual rate rise (Bajaj prints +8%/yr). **Its absence is worth more than a low entry price.**
> - **Zone** = you pay by the city you live in. Metros cost the most.
> - **GST** = **now 0%** on individual health (since 22 Sept 2025) — the quoted premium *is* the final price.

## 🆕 NEW DIMENSION discovered in this module *(Rule 3)*

### 1. Rate-chart transparency — can the premium curve be independently verified at all? *(→ new bullet in study_plan M4; new row above)*
Every M4 tool the framework has built — the **age-loading curve**, the **premium-per-lakh SI-efficiency curve**, the **pre-announced escalator check**, and the **10/20-year outgo projection** — silently assumes the insurer **publishes a rate chart**. Two finalists do, and it mattered enormously: **SBI's** chart let M4 catch a **16–32% premium mis-read** carried through two modules, and **Bajaj's** chart is the only reason its **contractual +8%/yr escalator** was ever discovered. **Care Health publishes none**, and pricing is gated behind a quote engine requiring personal contact details. The consequences are concrete: you **cannot verify the age curve before buying**, **cannot detect an escalator's presence or absence from the document**, and — most importantly for a 40-year hold — **cannot audit a future renewal hike against a published baseline**, because no baseline exists.
> **New generalisable check: "Does the insurer publish a public, verifiable premium rate chart? If not, treat every M4 figure as quote-dependent and unverifiable, flag the reduced evidence quality in the score, and note that the buyer will have no published baseline against which to challenge future repricing."** A plan whose price cannot be independently checked is **structurally less auditable over a multi-decade hold**, regardless of whether today's quote is cheap.

### *(Refinement to the existing "Discount stack" bullet)*
The framework lists discounts but never asks **how they combine**. Care states it explicitly: *"All discounts… are **multiplicative** in nature, subject to **aggregate maximum discount… not exceeding 50% of the Premium**."* **Multiplicative stacking is worth less than additive** (three 15% discounts = 38.6% off, not 45%), and the **50% aggregate cap** is the real ceiling on the discount stack. → **Ask: are discounts additive or multiplicative, and is there an aggregate cap?**

**Module 4 score (1–5): 3.5 / 5**

**Rationale.** On **economics** Care Supreme is genuinely well-structured for a 26-year-old, and three of its strengths are the ones that actually compound over a 40-year hold: **no contractually printed escalator** (the wording carries only the standard §5.14 revision power — against **Bajaj's printed +8%/yr**, which doubles base rates every ~9 years, this is the single most valuable pricing feature a plan can have); **verified 5-year age-banding** rather than annual re-pricing, proved from the binding wording itself (ages 56 and 60 both ₹31,113; 61 and 64 both ₹36,965); and **no claim-based renewal loading** (§5.10 iv). Add **GST now 0%** (the ~₹13,312 tier-1 quote at ₹15L age 25 *is* the final price, ~15% cheaper in real terms than the old GST-inclusive world, and competitive with Optima Secure+), **no pre-policy medical-test cliff at any SI up to age 65** (so the large SI carries no hidden underwriting cost — the trap that penalised SBI's ₹30L+ tiers), a **multiplicative discount stack capped at a generous 50%**, an **instalment option with grace-period cover**, and a **wellness earn-back of up to 30% structured as a discount rather than a forfeitable wallet**.

It is held to 3.5 by one structural weakness and three real frictions. The structural one is **transparency: Care publishes no rate chart at all** — uniquely among the finalists — so the age curve, the per-lakh SI-efficiency curve and the 10/20-year outgo **cannot be verified on public data**, and a buyer will have **no published baseline against which to contest a future repricing** under §5.14 (whose revision history is itself unverified). The frictions: **metro buyers sit in the dearest of 7 zones** (~28% above tier-3, versus SBI's genuinely zone-agnostic pricing); the **age-band steps are steep in middle age** (+26% / +33% / +19%, roughly doubling premium between 46 and 61); and the **instalment option carries the claim trap** — a claim makes all remaining instalments fall due and Care may deduct them from the payout, while the tenure discount is forfeited entirely. Finally, several headline "discounts" (co-pay, deductible, Smart Select, True Connect) are **risk transfers, not savings**, and M2 flagged their reversibility as an open question. **Net: a cheap, sanely-structured, escalator-free premium whose true long-run cost cannot be independently audited — and whose cheapness is partly financed by the claims friction M3 measured.**
