# Module 3 — Claims & Servicing

_Source: IRDAI FY24-25 data + **Care Supreme policy wording** (UIN **CHIHLIP27061V032627**, v03), **Sec 6.1 Claims procedure and management** + Sec 5.3 (settlement TAT) + 5.16 (grievances) + 5.22 (limitation) + Annexure II (excluded providers). Files in `resources/`._
_Profile studied: **Individual (single adult), age 26, metro tier-1**_
_**Weight: 25% — the highest.** A plan that won't pay is worthless regardless of features._

> **Plain-English intro.** This module answers three questions: **"Will they pay? Will they pay fast? Can I go cashless near my home?"** The jargon you need:
> - **ICR (Incurred Claims Ratio)** = of every ₹100 of premium collected, how much went back out as claims. Low can mean *stingy* — **or** just cheap pricing / a fast-growing book. **Never read it alone, and never against the wrong benchmark** (see the new dimension below).
> - **CSR (Claim Settlement Ratio)** = what % of claims they paid, **counted as claims** (not rupees).
> - **Complaint ratio** = how many policyholders formally complained per 10,000 claims. **The hardest number to explain away** — it measures lived experience, not accounting.
>
> **This module carries Care's single carry-forward flag** from screening: *"ICR 64.5% + above-average complaints — young book or stingy culture?"* **It is resolved below — one half favourably, one half against.**

|--------------|---------|---------|
| Is Care a **young book**? | ❌ **No.** Care Health has operated **since 2012 (14 years)** and has settled **74.5 lakh+ claims**. This is a mature, high-volume insurer — not a startup whose claims haven't matured | **Theory rejected** |
| Is the low ICR evidence of stinginess? | ⚠️ **Largely no, once benchmarked correctly.** Against **SAHI peers (68.06%)** Care's 64.53% is only ~3.5 pts low — not the 20-pt outlier the all-industry average implies. ## 🆕 NEW DIMENSIONS discovered in this module *(Rule 3)*

### 1. Benchmark ICR against the correct PEER SEGMENT, not the all-industry average *(→ new bullet in study_plan M3; new row above)*
The framework says to pull ICR and compare it — but never says **to what**. That omission nearly mis-resolved this flag. The **all-industry health ICR (84–87%)** is **dragged upward by PSU general insurers running 99.84%**, while **standalone health insurers (SAHIs) as a segment run 68.06%**. Judging a SAHI against the all-industry number therefore **overstates its shortfall by ~16 points**. Care at **64.53% looks catastrophic against 84.38% but merely slightly-below-average against its true peer group's 68.06%** — and the same correction applies to Niva Bupa (61.22%) and to any future SAHI. Conversely a *general* insurer should not be flattered by the SAHI average. → **New generalisable check: "Compare each insurer's ICR to its OWN segment (SAHI / private general / PSU), not the blended industry figure — the blend is dominated by PSU insurers at ~100% ICR and will systematically make every SAHI look stingy."** *(Also record the ICR **trend**: a low-but-rising ICR signals a maturing book, not tightening claims.)*

### 2. Claim intimation drafted as a Condition Precedent — the "notification trap" *(→ new bullet in study_plan M3; new row above)*
The framework's M3 already extracts *"cashless pre-auth deadlines; reimbursement windows"* — but only as **timings**, never asking the decisive legal question: **is missing the window merely procedural, or is it drafted as a *condition precedent to liability* that can defeat an otherwise valid claim?** Care states it expressly: *"6.1.4(i) and 6.1.4(ii) are precedent to admission of liability."* So a fully covered ₹20L surgery can in principle be resisted because the insured called on day 3 instead of day 2. SBI, by contrast, carries an explicit condonation clause. → **New generalisable check: "For every intimation/filing deadline, check whether the wording makes it a *condition precedent to liability*, and whether any condonation/'reasons beyond control' relief exists. A claim-defeating notification clause is a materially worse deal than the same deadline drafted as procedural."**

**Module 3 score (1–5): 3.0 / 5**

**Rationale.** The good news is real and was under-appreciated at screening: Care **approves claims at a high rate** — **CSR 96.74% (3-yr 93.13%, above the 91.22% industry)** across **74.5 lakh+ claims since 2012** — settles **in-house** (no TPA hand-off), and the wording carries **contractual, not marketing, obligations**: a **15-day settle-or-reject TAT with 2%-above-bank-rate penal interest**, a **48-hour planned pre-auth window more forgiving than SBI's 72**, and a documented Ombudsman/IGMS escalation path. Critically, **the ICR half of the carry-forward flag largely dissolves under correct benchmarking**: against **SAHI peers at 68.06%** (not the PSU-inflated 84% all-industry blend) Care's **64.53% is only ~3.5 points low**, and it has **risen 10.7 points in three years** — a maturing, fast-growing book, not a tightening one. The "young book" theory is simply wrong: Care is 14 years old.

It scores only 3.0 because the module's other findings are genuinely poor, and M3 carries the study's heaviest weight. **The complaint ratio is the study's worst by a wide margin — 42 per 10,000 claims against an industry 27.06, and roughly 8.4× HDFC ERGO's 4.99** — and unlike the ICR, **no structural excuse (book age, growth, segment, pricing) explains a complaint ratio**. Read beside the **CSR-vs-ICR divergence** (approve almost everything by count, return far fewer rupees), it points to **claims *friction over the amount*** — precisely the levers M2 catalogued (R&C and Medically-Necessary discretion, the lifestyle-attributable exclusion, RMO/service/night charges, consumables sold only as a rider). Add the **weakest network of the three same-source metro tests (Mumbai 299 vs SBI 434 vs HDFC 520; per-district ≈14.6–15.3, below every leader)**, a **network Care may unilaterally restrict** (6.1.2 a vii) with an excluded-provider blacklist, a **claim-intimation clause drafted as a condition precedent to liability**, a **2.3× gap between marketed "22,000+" and self-published ~11,400** hospitals, and **unverifiable payout-by-amount and TAT-ageing data**, and the honest read is: **a company that will very probably pay your claim, but is the one you are most likely to have to argue with about how much — and whose network is the thinnest of the finalists where this buyer lives.** Buying the **Claim Shield rider (M1)** measurably reduces that argument surface.
