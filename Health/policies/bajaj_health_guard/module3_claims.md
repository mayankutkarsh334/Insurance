_Source: IRDAI FY2024-25 disclosures + insurer data + binding wording (UIN BAJHLIP25035V072425, `resources/`), Section E (Claims). Claims metrics are **insurer-level** (Bajaj General / Bajaj Allianz), not plan-specific._
_Profile: Individual, age 26, metro tier-1 (Zone A) · SI ₹10L–₹1Cr_

> **Why this module carries the most weight (25%).** A policy that looks great on paper is worthless if the insurer fights or slow-walks your claim. Module 3 asks the only question that matters when you're in a hospital bed: **"Will they pay, in full, and fast?"** Three evidence sources: (1) **IRDAI claim/complaint data** (their real track record), (2) the **wording's claim mechanics** (the deadlines you and they must meet), and (3) **network reach** (where you can go cashless). Bajaj is the study's designated *claims-reliability leader* — this module tests that claim.

### What the ICR number means (plain English)
```
ICR = claims paid ÷ premiums collected.  Read it as "how generously they pay."
  <60%  ▓░░░░  stingy / very profitable for insurer → claims culture suspect (e.g. Care 64.5% — a Stage-2 flag)
 70-90% ▓▓▓▓░  HEALTHY band — pays well, still sustainable      ← Bajaj 87.31% sits here, near the top
  >100% ▓▓▓▓▓  paying out more than it earns → future premium hikes / unsustainable
Bajaj's rising 74→85→87 over 3 yrs = a claims culture getting MORE generous, not tightening. Good signal.
```

> **Finding 1 — Bajaj leads the finalists on the two signals that most predict "will they pay":** the **highest ICR (87.31%, and rising)** and the **lowest complaint ratio (3.42 vs HDFC 9.28 per 10k claims)**. Low complaints = fewer disputes, rejections and delays in practice. This validates the Stage-2 "claims-reliability leader" tag.

## Claim mechanics (from binding wording)

| Item | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Cashless pre-auth deadline (planned / emergency) | **Planned: intimate 48 h before**; **Emergency: intimate within 24 h** of hospitalisation. Cashless **only at network hospitals** |
| Reimbursement intimation & doc windows | Inform **within 48 h** (emergency) / 48 h prior (planned); **submit documents within 30 days of discharge** |
| **Settlement TAT** | **Settle or reject within 30 days** of last necessary document; **45 days if investigation** is warranted |
| Penal interest for delay | **2% above bank rate**, from last-document date to payment date |
| **TPA vs in-house adjudication** | ✅ **In-house claims team — no TPA** (single point, faster, less document ping-pong). Company reserves the right to engage a TPA later |
| IRDAI 2024 mandate delivery (cashless-anywhere, 1 h / 3 h) | Participates in **Cashless Everywhere**; in-house team targets fast (≈1 h) cashless auth and 3-h discharge. Real-world delivery **corroborated by the lowest complaint ratio of the finalists** — no adverse ombudsman pattern found |
| Cashless-outside-India / 100% cashless | ❌ **No overseas cover/cashless** (Health Guard is domestic-only, Exclusion 23). Pushing toward 100%-cashless domestically |

> **Finding 3 — two small wording gaps vs the HDFC benchmark:**
> 1. **Contractual settlement TAT is 30 days** (45 with investigation) — **slower on paper than HDFC's 15-day** commitment. In practice cashless is fast, but the *contract* promises less.
> 2. The wording still frames pre-auth around "48 h before planned" and "30-day settlement" — it **hasn't been re-drafted to spell out the IRDAI-2024 1-hour-cashless / 3-hour-discharge mandate** (a wording-vs-regulation lag). The mandate still binds Bajaj operationally; the low complaint data suggests they meet it, but the *wording* understates the regulatory floor.

## SI-scaling read
- Claims/servicing metrics are **insurer-level → identical across every SI tier.** A ₹10L and a ₹1Cr Health Guard Platinum are adjudicated by the same in-house team, same 30-day TAT, same network.
- Only practical SI effect: **larger (high-SI) claims draw more investigation** (the 45-day investigation window), but Module 2 showed no co-pay and full-SI parity for modern treatments/mental illness, so admissible deductions stay low.
