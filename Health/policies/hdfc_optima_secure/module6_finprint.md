> **Read this first — what is "fine print & longevity"?**
> This is the small print that decides whether the insurer can **drop you, deny you late, or short-change a refund** years from now. For a policy you'll hold for decades these clauses matter as much as the benefits — they're the difference between *"covered for life"* and *"covered until it's inconvenient for them."*

## Plain-English glossary
| Term | In simple words |
|------|-----------------|
| **Lifelong renewability** | They must let you renew every year for life — they can't drop you for getting old or sick |
| **Moratorium** | After 5 years they can **no longer question your old disclosures** — your cover becomes near-unquestionable (except proven fraud) |
| **Grace period** | Extra days to pay a late renewal without losing your policy or waiting-period credit. ⚠️ **Not the same as being covered — see below** |
| **Free-look** | A 30-day "try it and return it" window right after you buy |
| **Portability / migration** | Your right to switch insurer or plan **without losing** the waiting periods you've already served |
| **Claim-based loading** | Hiking *your* premium because you claimed — **banned** by IRDAI |
| **Condition precedent** | A duty you must perform, or they can refuse the claim |

## 🚩 The grace-period gap — this was missed originally

The original module recorded the grace period simply as *"continuity preserved if paid within grace."* **That is only half the clause.**

> **Def. 17:** *"Grace Period means the specified period of time… during which premium payment can be made to renew or continue a policy in force **without loss of continuity benefits** pertaining to waiting periods and coverage of pre-existing diseases. **Coverage need not be available during the period for which no premium is received.**"*

```
   Renewal due date
        |
        v
  ======|============ 30-day GRACE PERIOD ============|==========
        |  X NOT COVERED (annual/quarterly payers)    |  covered
        |  OK Continuity + waiting-period credit KEPT |  again
        |                                             |
        |  A hospitalisation HERE is not payable      |
        |                                             |
        +-- but INSTALMENT payers ARE covered here ---+
```

> **Plain English: being "in grace" protects your *credits*, not your *cover*.** If you're hospitalised during those 30 days having not yet paid, the claim is not payable. ⚠️ **And the same perverse asymmetry found in Care and ACKO applies here: the wording expressly states that if premium is paid in instalments, coverage IS available during the grace period** — so the **annual payer, who gets the long-term discount, has the worse deal.**
>
> ✅ **Mitigants:** HDFC also carries the **Cumulative Bonus / Plus Benefit forfeiture rule** if you miss grace entirely, so the incentive to renew on time is strong; and **this gap is universal across Care, ACKO and HDFC** — it is not a differentiator, but it *is* a real risk the original module didn't state. **Practical rule: renew on or before the due date; treat grace as credit protection, not cover.**

## Module 6 score: **5 / 5** *(unchanged after re-testing)*

**Rationale.** Re-tested against six later dimensions, **this remains the cleanest fine print in the study** — and the three newest checks, the ones that damaged ACKO and Care, all come back **clean**. HDFC gives the insurer **no subjective escape hatch**: renewal may be refused only on the **three objective grounds** (fraud, misrepresentation, non-disclosure), where **ACKO permits refusal for undefined *"moral hazard"* and *"non-cooperation"* and can terminate mid-term on the latter**. It imposes **no continuing material-change condition precedent**, where **Care makes notifying a change of address or occupation a condition precedent to liability** — a live 40-year trap. And it contains **no arbitration clause at all**, where **ACKO makes an arbitral award a precondition to suing** on exactly the quantum disputes most likely to arise. Add **lifelong renewability with no maximum entry age**, a **60-month moratorium**, **30-day grace and free-look**, **pro-rata cancellation refunds** whose forfeiture trigger is a *claim* rather than Care's and ACKO's broader *"any benefit availed"*, **full portability and migration with bonus and waiting-period credit intact**, **90 days' notice on withdrawal**, and a **policyholder-favourable multiple-policy clause**. Uniquely among the finalists it also offers a **three-option non-disclosure remedy ladder** — permanently exclude, add waiting, or load — rather than **Care's and ACKO's binary "void and forfeit all premium"**, so an honest mistake need not destroy the policy.

Two real frictions keep it from being flawless, one newly found. **The grace-period coverage gap was missed originally**: Def. 17 states plainly that *"coverage need not be available during the period for which no premium is received"*, so the 30 days protect your **credits but not your cover** — and, perversely, **instalment payers are covered while annual payers are not**. **The instalment claim trap is present in its harsher "shall" form** — a claim accelerates the year's remaining instalments and nets them off your payout, undercutting the no-cost-EMI benefit of M4 (**ACKO's equivalent says "may"**). Both are real, but the grace gap is **universal across Care, ACKO and HDFC**, and neither approaches veto territory. **No override veto — and unlike ACKO, not even a near-miss.**
