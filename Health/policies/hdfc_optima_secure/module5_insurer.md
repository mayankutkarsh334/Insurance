# Module 5 — Insurer Quality

_Source: IRDAI + rating agencies (CRISIL/ICRA) + company financials. About the **company** (HDFC ERGO), not the specific plan._

> **Read this first — why does the company matter?**
> You'll hold this policy for **decades** and may make your biggest claim 20–30 years from now. So the question isn't just "is the plan good?" — it's "**will this company still exist, stay solvent, and pay fairly** that far out?" This module checks the insurer's financial strength, backing, and track record.

## Plain-English glossary
| Term | In simple words |
|------|-----------------|
| **Solvency ratio** | A safety cushion: how many times over the insurer can cover all the claims it's expected to owe. Law requires **at least 1.5×**; higher = safer |
| **Promoter / backing** | The big companies that own it and would stand behind it |
| **Reinsurer** | The "insurer's insurer" — takes on part of the risk so one disaster can't sink the company (here, Munich Re) |
| **Credit rating (AAA)** | An independent grade of financial strength. **AAA = the highest**, safest tier |
| **SAHI vs general insurer** | SAHI = health-only company. General insurer = does health *plus* car, travel, home, etc. |
| **RoE / combined ratio** | Is the company profitable? Profitable insurers feel less pressure to deny claims or hike prices |

## SI-scaling read
- Insurer strength is **the same whatever SI tier you buy** — a ₹10L and a ₹1Cr policyholder are backed by the same balance sheet, the same Munich Re reinsurance, and the same AAA rating.
- The only indirect link: **very large claims** (high-SI) lean harder on solvency and reinsurance — where HDFC ERGO's Munich Re backing and AAA rating are most reassuring.
