# Module 3 — Claims & Servicing

_Source: IRDAI FY2024-25 + insurer disclosures + binding wording `Activ One` (E.1.9, Section E). Claims metrics are **insurer-level** (Aditya Birla Health), not plan-specific._

> **Read this first — plain-English framing**
> This is the "will they actually pay, and quickly?" module. A cheap, feature-rich plan is worthless if the company drags its feet or disputes bills. We look at how much they pay out, how often people complain, how big the cashless hospital network is, and how fast the rules force them to settle.

## Plain-English glossary
| Term | In simple words |
|------|-----------------|
| **CSR (claim settlement ratio)** | Of all claims, the % they said "yes" to — by *number* |
| **ICR (incurred claim ratio)** | Of every ₹100 of premium collected, how much they paid back as claims. Very low = stingy; very high = unsustainable |
| **Cashless** | The hospital bills the insurer directly — you don't pay and wait for reimbursement |
| **In-house vs TPA** | In-house = insurer's own team decides claims (usually faster); TPA = an outsourced middleman |
| **Network density** | Cashless hospitals per district — a fairer measure than a big raw number |

|------|--------|

## Module 3 score: **4 / 5**
Strong, health-focused servicing: **CSR 95.88% (3-yr ~96%)**, below-average complaints, in-house claims, and **rank-#2 network density (~15.8/district)** — a clear notch behind the HDFC benchmark but comfortably good. The one watch-point is the **lower ICR (68% vs HDFC 85%)**: it's within the healthy band and paired with a high CSR, so it reads as an efficiently-priced growing book rather than a stingy one — but it means AB retains more of each premium rupee than HDFC does.
