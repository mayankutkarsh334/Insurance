# Module 4 — Cost & Premium

_Source: **Acko Personal Health Policy wording** (UIN **ACKHLIP25035V022425**) — §5.1.15 instalments, **§5.1.16 revision of premium rates**, §5.1.17 free look, §5.2.3 zones, §5.2.4 premium, §3.3.4–3.3.7 discount levers; published quote anchors. Files in `resources/`._
_Profile studied: **Individual (single adult), age 26, metro tier-1 (Zone A — Delhi/NCR, Mumbai, Kolkata)**_
_Studied across SI tiers: **₹10L / ₹25L / ₹50L / ₹1Cr**_

> **Plain-English intro.** This module asks: **"What does it cost me — this year, and for the next 40 years?"** The traps live in how the price *grows*:
> - **Escalator** = a contractually printed annual rate rise (Bajaj prints +8%/yr). **Its absence is worth more than a low entry price.**
> - **Age band** = does the price step up every 5 years (gentler) or every single year (harsher)?
> - **Zone** = you pay by where you live. Metros cost most.
> - **GST** = **now 0%** on individual health — the quoted premium *is* the final price.

## ⚠️ METHOD NOTE — Acko's price is **algorithmic**, so no rate chart exists *even in principle*

Care Supreme (M4) failed the framework's rate-chart transparency test because it **doesn't publish** its filed rates. **Acko fails it more fundamentally: there is nothing tabular to publish.** Acko states it uses an **"AI-based pricing engine"** generating a **personalised premium per applicant** from age, PIN code, sum insured, plan variant, medical history, family composition and underwriting outcome — and its own aggregators confirm *"You will not find a single static ACKO Health Insurance premium chart because premiums are personalised for every applicant."*

## 🆕 NEW DIMENSION discovered in this module *(Rule 3)*

### Algorithmic / personalised pricing vs filed tabular rates — the price you cannot audit *(→ new bullet in study_plan M4, extending the Care M4 rate-chart bullet)*
Care M4 established that a plan whose **rate chart is unpublished** cannot be audited. **Acko exposes the sharper form of the problem: pricing that is *algorithmic*, so no rate table exists even in principle.** Acko's premium is generated per applicant by an **"AI-based pricing engine"** from age, PIN code, SI, variant, medical history, family composition and underwriting outcome. Four consequences the framework had no place to record:
1. **No baseline to contest a renewal hike.** With a filed table you can point at the published rate for your age band; with an algorithm there is nothing to point at.
2. **Like-for-like comparison breaks down.** A single quote is one draw from a personalised function — it may not generalise even to a near-identical buyer.
3. **Two identical-risk buyers can legitimately pay different prices**, on inputs neither can see or challenge.
4. **The framework's own M4 toolkit degrades** — the age-loading curve, per-lakh efficiency curve and 10/20-year outgo all assume a stable published schedule.
> **New generalisable check: "Is the insurer's pricing TABULAR (a filed rate chart, published or obtainable) or ALGORITHMIC/personalised? Algorithmic pricing is the least auditable form: mark every M4 figure as a single personalised draw rather than a schedule, treat cross-plan per-lakh comparisons as indicative only, and note that the buyer has no published baseline to challenge future re-pricing. Where pricing is algorithmic, a LONG-TERM (2–3 yr) premium lock becomes disproportionately valuable, because it is the only way to fix the price at all."**

**Module 4 score (1–5): 3.5 / 5**

**Rationale.** On raw economics Acko is **in a different league from every other plan studied**: **₹16,178 buys ₹1 crore of cover at age 25 in Delhi — ₹162 per lakh**, roughly **3× more efficient than SBI's best tier and 5.5× more than Care Supreme**, so that **for about what Care charges for ₹15 lakh, Acko sells ₹1 crore**. Crucially the mechanism is **verifiable and legitimate rather than a teaser**: Acko is **direct-to-consumer with zero agent commission**, stripping out the ~15% distribution load Care's own prospectus discloses paying. It also carries the **strongest re-pricing protections in the study** — **no printed escalator** (against Bajaj's contractual +8%/yr), and a §5.1.16 revision clause requiring **prior IRDAI approval with three months' notice**, both stronger than Care's bare "notified before" — plus the option to **fix the premium for 2–3 years via a long-term policy**, **no claim-based renewal loading**, **GST at 0%**, only **two pricing zones with no zone-co-pay trap**, and an instalment claim clause that is **discretionary ("may") where Care's is mandatory ("shall")**.

It is held to 3.5 by one structural weakness and three real frictions. The structural one is **auditability: Acko's pricing is algorithmic and personalised, so no rate chart exists even in principle** — the buyer has **no published baseline to contest a future increase**, cross-plan comparisons rest on single personalised draws, and **the requested 26/35/45/55/65/75 age curve across four SI tiers simply cannot be built from public data**. The frictions: **age-banding is absent and pricing is almost certainly annual on attained age** — the weaker model, and a direct contrast with Care's **5-year banding proven from its own wording**; the only age signal available (**~4.55× from ages 31 to 62, ≈5%/yr**) is derived from **family-floater** anchors and is flagged as an assumption; there is **no wellness earn-back at all**, where Care and SBI both offer up to 30% off renewal; and **four separate discount levers are risk transfers rather than savings**, moving cost to claim time. **Exceptional price and the best revision protections in the study, attached to the least auditable pricing in the study.**
