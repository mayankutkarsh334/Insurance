# Module 6 — Fine Print & Longevity

_Source: **Acko Personal Health Policy wording** (UIN **ACKHLIP25035V022425**, v02) — **Section 5.1.1–5.1.21 General Terms** and **§5.2.1–5.2.13 Specific Terms**, §6.2 Grievance Redressal, def. 32 (Moratorium), def. 40 (Portability). Files in `resources/`._
_Profile studied: **Individual (single adult), age 26, metro tier-1** — a **40–60 year** relationship._
_**Weight: 5% — but this module holds the OVERRIDE VETO.** A deal-breaker here disqualifies the plan regardless of total._

> **Plain-English intro.** You are 26. This policy may need to be paying claims when you are 70. This module asks: **"Can they ever throw me out? Can they quietly change the deal? What small print could cost me a claim decades from now?"**
> - **Renewability** = can they refuse to renew you? (Must be *lifelong*.)
> - **Moratorium** = after 5 continuous years they can no longer dig into your past to contest a claim.
> - **Grace period** = days after your due date to still pay. ⚠️ **Being "in grace" is not the same as being covered.**

## 🚩 The two clauses that separate Acko from every rival

```
  RENEWAL-DENIAL GROUNDS — the wording says two different things

  Section 5.1.14                          Section 5.2.11
  ------------------------------          ---------------------------------
  - established fraud                     - misrepresentation
  - non-disclosure                        - MORAL HAZARD          <- undefined
  - misrepresentation                     - fraud
                                          - non-disclosure
                                          - NON-COOPERATION       <- undefined
        (3 objective grounds)                   (5 grounds, 2 subjective)

  And Section 5.2.12(b) lets Acko TERMINATE MID-TERM for "non-cooperation".

  Care Supreme / SBI:  misrepresentation, non-disclosure, fraud only.
```

> **Why this matters in plain English.** "Lifelong renewability" is only as strong as the list of reasons the insurer may refuse. Acko's own wording gives **two different lists**, and the longer one includes **"moral hazard"** and **"non-cooperation"** — **neither of which is defined anywhere in the policy**. "Non-cooperation" is doubly significant because §5.2.12(b) also makes it a ground to **cancel your policy in the middle of a year**, which Care and SBI cannot do.
>
> **Does this trigger the override veto? No — but it is the closest call in the study.** Renewal remains the **default**, claiming is **expressly excluded** as a ground, and §5.1.14(6) blocks fresh underwriting. These read as broadened versions of standard fraud/misrepresentation carve-outs rather than a licence to drop unprofitable policyholders. **But they are subjective, undefined, and unilateral — and a buyer should treat them as the plan's most important open risk over a 40-year hold.**

## 🆕 NEW DIMENSIONS discovered in this module *(Rule 3)*

### 1. Undefined subjective grounds for non-renewal or mid-term termination *(→ new bullet in study_plan M6)*
The framework tests **"lifelong renewability — must be YES"** as a binary, and separately checks claim-based loading. **It never examines the LIST of grounds on which renewal may still be refused, or whether those grounds are objective.** Acko exposes why that matters: **§5.2.11 permits refusal for *"moral hazard"* and *"non-cooperation"*, and §5.2.12(b) permits mid-term termination for *"non-cooperation"* — none of these defined anywhere in the wording** — while **§5.1.14 lists only the three objective grounds** (fraud, non-disclosure, misrepresentation). A plan can therefore **satisfy the veto test on paper while retaining a broad, undefined, unilateral discretion to exit the relationship.**
> **New generalisable check: "Extract the FULL list of grounds for (a) refusing renewal and (b) mid-term cancellation, and test each for objectivity. Undefined standards such as 'moral hazard', 'non-cooperation' or 'moral turpitude' materially weaken a lifelong-renewability guarantee even where the veto is not formally triggered. Where the wording gives two different lists in two clauses, treat the discrepancy as a live drafting defect and get the insurer's position in writing."**

### 2. Arbitration as a condition precedent to suit — a redress barrier *(→ new bullet in study_plan M6)*
The framework maps the **Grievance → Ombudsman → IRDAI IGMS** escalation path but **never asks whether the wording imposes an additional, costly hurdle**. Acko's **§5.1.12** requires quantum disputes to go to **arbitration**, and expressly makes an arbitral award *"a **condition precedent to any right of action or suit** upon the policy."* Arbitration costs an individual real money (arbitrator fees, legal representation) where the **Ombudsman is free**; and the clause is **asymmetric** — it does not apply where Acko denies liability outright, i.e. it binds you where the dispute is about *how much*, which M3 identified as the likeliest kind of dispute with a low-ICR insurer. **None of the other four finalists carries an arbitration clause.**
> **New generalisable check: "Does the wording impose ARBITRATION on the policyholder, and is an arbitral award drafted as a condition precedent to suing? Note whether the clause is symmetric (does it also bind the insurer where IT disputes liability?). Weigh it against the free statutory Ombudsman route, which cannot be contracted away — but treat a mandatory-arbitration clause as a real friction on redress, particularly for quantum disputes."**
