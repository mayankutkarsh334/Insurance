# Module 5 — Insurer Quality

_Source: **ACKO General Insurance Limited Annual Report FY2024-25** (audited; Schedule 1.12 Summary of Financial Statements and **Schedule 1.13 Ratio Analysis, both READ BY RENDERING THE PDF PAGES AS IMAGES per the METHOD RULE**), Directors' Report §21 & §38; IRDAI data. Files in `resources/`._
_Profile studied: **Individual (single adult), age 26, metro tier-1** — a **40+ year** relationship, so this module asks whether the *company* survives and stays fair._

> **Plain-English intro.** You're buying a promise that must hold for four decades. Three questions:
> - **Solvency ratio** = the safety buffer. IRDAI demands **at least 1.50**. Higher = safer; **the trend matters more than the level**.
> - **Combined ratio** = costs ÷ premium. **Over 100% means the insurer loses money on insurance.** But *why* it loses matters enormously — see the new dimension below.
> - **Promoter & rating** = who stands behind it, and what independent agencies think.

## 📊 The decisive chart — **why** Acko loses money

```
  ACKO FY25 combined ratio = 125.87%   (anything over 100% = underwriting loss)

    Net incurred claims ratio   ###############        69.95%   <- HEALTHY
    Expense of management       ############           55.92%   <- THE PROBLEM
                                ------------------------------
    Combined                                          125.87%

  Segment detail (FY25):
    Motor OD      combined 150.50%   claims 107.65%   <- the loss centre
    Motor TP      combined 111.41%   claims  66.47%
    Motor Total   combined 126.22%   claims  82.08%
    TOTAL         combined 125.87%   claims  69.95%   <- total claims BELOW motor's
                                                         => non-motor (incl. health)
                                                         claims LESS than motor
```

> ### ⭐ **The single most important finding in this module: Acko's loss is EXPENSE-driven, not CLAIMS-driven.**
> Its **claims ratio is 69.95% — entirely healthy**. What pushes the combined ratio to 125.87% is an **Expense of Management ratio of 47.71% of gross direct premium (55.92% of net written premium)** — the classic insurtech pattern of heavy customer-acquisition, technology and brand spend.
>
> **Why this matters for you, in plain English.** A company losing money because **claims exceed premium** has only two fixes — **charge more or pay less** — and both land on the policyholder. A company losing money because it **spends too much on marketing and technology** can fix it by **scaling and cutting costs**, which touches the policyholder not at all. **Acko is the second kind. Care Supreme (M5) was the first kind** — its FY25 deterioration was driven by claims growing 33% against 21% premium growth.
>
> ✅ **And the fix is already visibly working:** operating profit turned positive (+₹85.40 cr) and the combined ratio fell 3.4 points in a single year, while premium grew 10.4%. → **New dimension #1.**

### 🔎 Segment attribution *(framework — SBI M5)*
**The loss sits in MOTOR, not health.** Motor OD runs a **150.50% combined ratio** on a **107.65% claims ratio** — i.e. motor own-damage pays out more in claims than it collects in premium. The **total claims ratio of 69.95% is well BELOW motor's 82.08%**, which necessarily means **non-motor business (including health) runs a lower claims ratio** — consistent with M3's health ICR of 57.82%.
> ⚠️ **Health is NOT separately disclosed** — it sits inside "Total Miscellaneous" in Schedule 1.13, so a standalone **health combined ratio is unverified**. *Confirming source: IRDAI segment-wise public disclosures (Form NL-30 / segmental reporting).* **Same conclusion as SBI General: a motor-driven loss threatens a health policyholder only indirectly.**

### 📈 Retail-health share of the book *(framework — SBI M5)*
**Unverified at the retail level.** Gross Direct Premium **₹2,064.67 cr (FY25, +10.4%)**; the Directors' Report notes **motor grew 21% and health 20%**, so health is a **major and fast-growing line but not the dominant one** — motor is. *Confirming source: IRDAI segment-wise GDPI disclosures.* **Read: the buyer is neither the core customer (as at a SAHI) nor a rounding error — health is a strategic growth line for Acko.**

## 🆕 NEW DIMENSIONS discovered in this module *(Rule 3)*

### 1. Decompose the combined ratio — is the loss CLAIMS-driven or EXPENSE-driven? *(→ new bullet in study_plan M5)*
The framework asks *"is the loss in **your line**?"* (SBI M5) and *"how many **levers** does the insurer have?"* (Care M5) — but never asks the prior question: **what is actually causing the loss?** A combined ratio above 100% can be produced two entirely different ways, with **opposite consequences for the policyholder**:

| | **Claims-driven loss** (Care Supreme) | **Expense-driven loss** (Acko) |
|---|---|---|
| Signature | Claims ratio high; claims outgrowing premium (Care: +33% vs +21%) | **Claims ratio healthy (69.95%)**; expense ratio high (**47.71% EoM**) |
| Available fixes | **Raise prices** or **pay less** | **Scale up**, cut acquisition/marketing spend |
| Who absorbs it | 🚩 **The policyholder, necessarily** | ✅ **Shareholders — the policyholder is untouched** |
| Threat level | **Direct** | **Indirect** |

> **New generalisable check: "Split the combined ratio into its claims ratio and its expense ratio before judging a loss-making insurer. An EXPENSE-driven loss (typical of young insurtechs scaling on marketing and technology spend) is far less threatening to a policyholder than a CLAIMS-driven loss, because it can be fixed by growth and cost discipline rather than by repricing or tightening claims. Weight a 125% combined ratio built on a 70% claims ratio very differently from a 110% combined ratio built on a 95% claims ratio."**

### 2. VC/PE-backed insurers — solvency sustained by funding rounds rather than earnings *(→ new bullet in study_plan M5)*
Every ownership check in the framework assumes a **corporate, bank or family promoter** (Bajaj's Allianz exit, SBI's bank parent, Care's Burman takeover). **A venture-backed insurtech is a structurally different animal.** Acko has **burned ~₹2,000 cr of paid-up capital**, and its solvency ratio **oscillates with funding events** (1.68 → 2.75 → 1.89 → 2.30, rescued by ₹300 cr and ₹349 cr equity issues) rather than tracking retained earnings. The risks are distinctive: **(a) funding-market dependency** — a capital-markets freeze could strand solvency near the floor; **(b) exit pressure** — an IPO or PE exit creates pressure to demonstrate profitability, which *could* translate into repricing or claims tightening; **(c) no rated parent** to backstop, unlike SBI or Bajaj.
> **New generalisable check: "For a VC/PE-backed insurer, check whether each solvency recovery coincides with a FUNDING ROUND rather than with profits — plot solvency against equity issuance. Ask: how many years of current losses does the present capital fund? Is the insurer approaching operating break-even? And what does the sponsor's exit path (IPO, secondary sale) imply for future pricing and claims discipline?"** ✅ Applied to Acko: **funding-dependent, but at operating break-even with an IPO in motion — de-risking, not deteriorating.**

### 3. Score the LICENSED ENTITY, not the brand or the group *(→ new bullet in study_plan M5)*
Press coverage of insurtechs routinely quotes **holdco** numbers. Acko's widely-reported **"₹424 cr FY25 loss"** is **Acko Technology & Services**; the regulated insurer, **ACKO General Insurance Limited**, lost **₹193.38 cr** and turned its **first operating profit**. Using the group figure would have **overstated the insurer's loss by 2.2×** and missed the inflection entirely.
> **New generalisable check: "Identify the exact licensed entity on the policy (IRDAI registration number) and take solvency, combined ratio, net worth and profitability from THAT entity's audited annual report — not from group/holdco press coverage, which for insurtechs and conglomerates can differ by a multiple."**

**Module 5 score (1–5): 3.0 / 5**

**Rationale.** Acko's balance sheet is **cleaner than any finalist's on quality, and its trajectory is the best in the study**. Solvency is **2.30×** and — uniquely — **entirely core equity: no debt, no subordinated debt, nil NPAs**, so unlike SBI General (2.13× headline flattered by ₹700 cr of sub-debt, core 1.84×) there is nothing to strip out. FY25 marked a genuine inflection: **the first operating profit in the company's history (+₹85.40 cr, from −₹68.42 cr)**, the **net loss cut 58% to ₹193.38 cr**, and the **combined ratio down 3.4 points to 125.87%** on 10.4% premium growth. Decisively for a health buyer, **the loss is EXPENSE-driven, not claims-driven** — the claims ratio is a healthy **69.95%** while Expense of Management runs at **47.71%** — so the fix is scale and cost discipline borne by **shareholders**, not repricing or claims-tightening borne by **policyholders**. That is the opposite of Care Supreme, whose FY25 losses came from claims outgrowing premium. **Segment attribution confirms the damage sits in MOTOR** (Motor OD combined 150.50%, claims 107.65%), not health. Backing is credible — **General Atlantic, Amazon, CPPIB, Accel, Lightspeed and Munich Re Ventures**, ~$600 mn raised, and an **IPO at $2–2.5 bn in motion** with top-tier banks appointed.

It cannot score higher because the **structural risks are real and unusual for this study**. Acko is **~8 years old — by far the youngest insurer here — with no record through a full claims or stress cycle**, and it has **consumed roughly ₹2,000 crore of shareholder capital** (paid-up ₹2,796 cr against ₹796 cr net worth, RoNW −24.28%, never a dividend). **Its solvency is sustained by funding rounds rather than earnings** — it fell to **1.68× in FY22, within 0.18 of the regulatory floor**, and each recovery tracks a fresh equity issue (₹300 cr FY24, ₹349 cr FY25) — so a capital-markets freeze is a genuine, if currently remote, risk. It is also the **only plan in this study with no public credit or financial-strength rating** (against Care's `IND AA-`, SBI's `[ICRA]AAA` and Bajaj's `AAA`), carries an **IRDAI ₹1 crore penalty** for routing commission-like payments to Zipcash/Ola without Outsourcing Committee approval (FY20-21, under SAT appeal), and **does not disclose a health-specific combined ratio**. **A young, unrated, capital-hungry insurer with an unusually clean balance sheet and the most convincing improvement trajectory in the study — where the losses fall on shareholders rather than on the people making claims.**
