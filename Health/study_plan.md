# Deep Study Plan: 6-Module Framework

## Context
After Stage 1 + Stage 2 screening, **5 finalist plans** are shortlisted. Each finalist is studied across **6 modules** before a final weighted-scored comparison. Modules are worked **in dependency order** — you can't judge cost (M4) or fine print (M6) until you know what's covered (M1) and what's excluded (M2).

Primary source for Modules 1, 2, 6 is the **policy wording PDF** (not the brochure). Modules 3, 5 use **IRDAI data**. Module 4 uses the **live quote engine**.

---

## Definitions That Decide Claims *(read Section A of every wording first)*
Before scoring anything, extract each plan's **definitions** — these are the levers insurers use to cut or deny claims, and they vary between plans even when benefits look identical. From the Optima Secure+ wording, the ones that matter most:

| Definition | Why it decides claims | Optima Secure+ |
|------------|-----------------------|----------------|
| **Pre-existing disease** | Lookback window that defines what's "pre-existing" | 36-month lookback |
| **Any One Illness** | Relapse window — a return within X days is the *same* claim (affects deductible/restore) | 45-day relapse rule |
| **Reasonable & Customary charges** | Lets insurer trim a bill to "customary" rates | Present — discretionary |
| **Medically Necessary** | Lets insurer reject treatment it deems unnecessary | Present — discretionary |
| **Hospital / Day-care / Room categories** | Defines what qualifies for a claim at all | Standard IRDAI defs |

> **Add this as the first pass of Module 1/2 for every finalist.** Two plans with "no room cap" can still differ on R&C and Medically-Necessary discretion.

> **Also test the wording against *itself*** *(new — SBI M2)*: **compare Section A's definitions to the Product Benefit Table.** A boilerplate **definition ceiling can exceed the actual grid** — SBI's def. 49 permits a Specific Waiting Period *"up to 36 months (as per plan opted)"* while every rung's Annexure I actually says **24/12**. Not a live conflict (the schedule binds), but the **unused headroom can be occupied by a future variant or revision without a new UIN**. The framework already tests *brochure vs wording*; this tests *wording vs wording*. Note the headroom wherever a definition is looser than the grid.

## Regulatory Floor — IRDAI 2024 Entitlements *(benchmark every plan against these)*
The **IRDAI Master Circular on Health Insurance (29 May 2024)** and **"Cashless Everywhere" (23 Jan 2024)** guarantee a baseline every plan *must* meet. These aren't differentiators on paper — but **real-world delivery against them is**, and it's scored in Module 3. Check each finalist honours:

| Entitlement | The mandated floor |
|-------------|--------------------|
| **Cashless anywhere** | Cashless allowed at *any* hospital, not just the network |
| **Pre-auth speed** | Cashless authorisation decision within **1 hour** of request |
| **Discharge speed** | Final authorisation within **3 hours** of discharge request; **insurer bears any hospital charge for delay beyond 3 hrs** |
| **100% cashless goal** | Reimbursement only in exceptional cases |
| **Moratorium** | After **60 months**, no claim contestable except for fraud |
| **No claim-based renewal loading** | Renewal premium can't be hiked just because you claimed |
| **Mandated coverages** | Mental illness (since 31 Oct 2022), 12 modern treatments, day-care, AYUSH |
| **Portability continuity** | Waiting-period credit carries on port/migrate |

> Use as a **gate + a scoring lens**: any plan that under-delivers on the *speed* mandates (a common gap between circular and practice) loses Module 3 points; verify via recent policyholder/ombudsman reports, not the brochure.

## Coverage Architecture — Base vs Base + Super-Top-Up *(decide before scoring SI)*
For a **26-year-old**, the cheapest route to a large lifetime cover is often **not** one giant base policy. Two structures to compare per finalist:

| Structure | How it works | When it wins |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|
| **Single large base** (e.g. ₹50L–1Cr) | One policy, one premium | Simpler; full features on the whole SI; no deductible gap |
| **Moderate base + super top-up** (e.g. ₹10L base + ₹90L super top-up over ₹10L aggregate deductible) | Top-up pays once base/deductible is crossed; super top-up pays *multiple* claims past the deductible | Much cheaper per ₹ of high cover; base absorbs small claims, top-up catches catastrophes |

**Cautions to check in the study:** super top-up **deductible must be met** before it pays (a gap year-to-year); ensure **no room-rent cap / sub-limits on the top-up**; confirm base and top-up **waiting periods align**; a single insurer for both eases claims but split insurers add coordination risk (see M6 multiple-policies). Note each finalist's own super-top-up companion product (e.g. Aditya Birla *Super Health Plus*, HDFC *my:Optima Secure* top-up) and price the layered vs single-base cost.

## The 6 Modules

### Module 1 — Coverage & Benefits *(policy wording — Sections A & B)*
- Sum insured options chosen for study; **variant selection** (e.g. Optima Secure vs Select vs Suraksha vs Lite — each modifies room rent / PED / bonus)
- **Variant-ladder mis-selling check** *(new — HDFC M1)*: many products sell several plan variants under **one name + one UIN** with materially different room-rent/bonus. Confirm the schedule names the exact top variant you intend, not a cheaper capped sibling
- **Wording-FIXED vs Schedule-DELEGATED limits — how much does the contract actually guarantee?** *(new — Acko M1)*: the whole framework rests on *"brochure sells, wording binds"* — which assumes **the wording fixes the numbers**. **Acko breaks that premise.** Its *Personal Health Policy* (UIN ACKHLIP25035V022425) delegates limits to the Schedule in **37 places** — **room rent, ICU, PED waiting period, co-payment %, pre/post-hospitalisation days, modern-treatment limits** — and makes **restore, bonus, consumables cover and even the initial-waiting-period waiver into options**. Worse, the marketed plan name **"Platinum" appears nowhere in the 57-page wording**; it is a marketing bundle, and a cheaper "Platinum Lite" bundle shares the same UIN. **Consequences: you cannot verify the product pre-purchase from the contract; two buyers of the "same" plan can hold different cover; and the insurer can re-configure future offerings without a new UIN.** **Ask: count the material limits the wording FIXES versus DELEGATES. Where delegation is heavy, the SCHEDULE OF BENEFITS is the real contract — obtain and read it before purchase, and treat "no room cap"-type claims as configuration settings, not guarantees.** *(Contrast Care Supreme, whose wording states the eligible room is "No limit" as the default — a promise, not a setting.)*
- **Room rent** — capped, no-cap, or "any room"? Proportionate-deduction risk? (Optima Secure+: at actuals)
- **Room-eligibility gated by SI band** *(new — Bajaj M1)*: some plans grant "any room / no cap" **only above an SI threshold** and cap you to a single A/C room below it (Bajaj Health Guard: single-A/C-room at ₹3–7.5L, **any room only at ₹10L+**). Don't assume "no room cap" is universal across the plan's SI ladder — confirm it applies at *your* SI
- **Benefit-stacking math** — how Base SI + Secure (2x day-1) + Infinite (100%/yr) + Restore actually combine, and the **per-claim cap rule** (a single claim can't exceed Base + remaining Secure + remaining Infinite)
- **Multiplier hard-cap check** *(new — Aditya Birla MAX M1)*: a headline "grows X×" bonus can carry a **flat-rupee ceiling** that clips it at higher SI (e.g. ABHI Super Credit "6×" is capped at ₹3Cr, so the 6× is only fully delivered up to ~₹60L base — at ₹1Cr it's ~4×). Check whether any day-1 multiplier / infinite bonus / restore carries such a cap, and at which SI it starts to bite
- **Bonus-ceiling inversion / bonus-type swap across the ladder** *(new — SBI M1)*: distinct from the flat-rupee cap above. Some products **change the bonus *engine itself* between variants**, so cover-growth can get *worse* as SI rises. SBI Super Health: Platinum (₹10–50L) gives a claim-dependent ECB capped at **200%**, but the ₹1Cr buyer is **forced onto Platinum Infinite, which has NO ECB at all** — only a claim-proof Loyalty Credit capped at **100%**. The top tier therefore has the *weakest* ceiling and no way to opt back. **Ask: does the bonus change type or ceiling across the SI/variant ladder, and does the top rung lose something?** Note the trade honestly — a claim-*proof* 100% may beat a claim-*erodable* 200% for a claim-prone buyer
- **Condition-gated SI multiplier** *(new — SBI M1)*: the "instant multiplier" row implicitly assumes an **unconditional** multiplier (HDFC's 2× day-1 applies to any claim). But some are **gated to a named illness list** — SBI's "Health Multiplier" is 2–3× **only for 37 listed Serious Illnesses**, triggers **once per year**, **can't be carried forward**, and is **absent entirely on the Prime rung**. A gated 3× is worth far less than an unconditional 2×. **Ask: is the multiplier unconditional, or gated to a list/trigger — and is it present on the rung you're actually buying?**
- **Voluntary room-cap / sub-limit modifier sold as a discount** *(new — SBI M1; also seen in Bajaj M1)*: a plan can truthfully advertise "no room cap" while **selling an opt-in cap** that re-enables proportionate deduction in exchange for cheaper premium (Bajaj: 8% discount for a 1.5%-of-SI cap; SBI: `Room Rent – 2%, ICU – 4%` "Base Cover Modifiers"). **Ask: does such a modifier exist, and does the schedule silently carry it?** Don't opt it if the no-cap benefit is the reason you chose the plan
- **Voluntary co-pay / network-gatekeeping modifier sold as a discount** *(new — Care Supreme M1; a second class alongside the room-cap modifier above)*: distinct from a *benefit* cap — this attaches a **percentage co-pay** or a **network/gatekeeper condition** that only bites at claim time, so it's invisible on the certificate's benefit grid. Care Supreme sells **two**: *Smart Select* (20% co-pay if treated outside a preferred-hospital list) and *True Connect* (10% co-pay unless a non-emergency claim is first routed through an empaneled physician). **Ask: does the plan sell a voluntary co-pay or network-gatekeeping modifier for a discount, and does the schedule silently carry it?** (Also feeds M4 net-cost.) ⚠️ **Extension (Acko M1) — the NOTIFICATION-LINKED co-pay, a third sub-type.** Acko's **§3.3.4 "First Notification of Claim"** gives a premium discount for agreeing to notify within **48 hours of hospitalisation**, and **applies a compulsory co-payment to the claim if you fail** — converting an **administrative deadline into a priced penalty**. (Its §3.3.5 Preferred Providers Network is the ordinary network-gatekeeping type.) **Instructive contrast: Care Supreme makes the same 48-hour intimation a *condition precedent to liability* — miss it and the claim can be refused ENTIRELY — whereas Acko prices it as a proportionate co-pay. Acko's construction is the fairer of the two.** → **Ask: does any discount lever attach a penalty to a PROCEDURAL failure (notification, network routing) rather than to a medical cost-share — and is that penalty proportionate (co-pay) or total (condition precedent)?**
- Restoration / recharge — how many times, same illness or unrelated? (Optima: automatic, unlimited)
- **Cumulative Bonus** — accrual rate, ceiling, and whether a claim reduces it (Infinite Benefit accrues *irrespective of claims*)
- Pre / post-hospitalisation days (Optima: 60 / 180)
- Day-care, domiciliary, home healthcare, AYUSH, organ-donor, road/air ambulance, daily cash for shared room
- **Day-1 cover for listed chronic conditions** *(new — Aditya Birla M1)*: some plans cover named chronic conditions (diabetes, hypertension, high cholesterol, COPD, etc.) **from day 1 with zero waiting** — a major plus for anyone with a lifestyle condition; check whether it's inbuilt or a paid optional
- **Non-medical / consumables cover** (Protect Benefit) — check against the exhaustive Non-Medical Expenses list in the wording. **Economics: consumables run ₹15k–₹1L per admission** (higher for ICU/surgery) — a plan that excludes them has a large silent gap
- Preventive health check-up sub-limits; e-opinion; global/emergency cover
- **Wellness / rewards as real value** — HealthReturns / earn-back-premium, fitness/gym benefits, discounts for healthy activity (material for a 26-yo; e.g. Aditya Birla HealthReturns). Note but weigh only if it reduces *net* cost
- Maternity / OPD / add-ons — note but down-weight

### Module 2 — Exclusions & Waiting Periods *(policy wording — Section C)*
- Initial 30-day waiting exceptions
- **PED waiting** (Optima: 36 mo) + PED-waiting-**modification** options; note waiting reduces 1 yr per continuous year. *(new — HDFC M2: check **who** can opt the reduction — Optima's 36→24/12-mo cut is **channel-level only, NOT available to individual buyers**; a reduction that exists on paper may be unreachable)*
- **Specific-disease waiting** — extract the *actual list* (Optima: 24 mo — cataract, hernia, joint replacement, ENT, D&C, etc.)
- **Is the specific-disease list CLOSED or OPEN (extensible via the Schedule)?** *(new — Acko M2)*: the bullet above says to *"extract the actual list"* — which silently assumes **the list is closed**. For Care (14 heads), SBI (43 items) and Bajaj (three fixed tiers) it is. **Acko's §4.1.2(f) item 18 breaks that assumption**: *"**Any other condition or treatment mentioned under this head in the Schedule** will have a waiting period **as specified in the Schedule**."* Combined with §4.1.1/§4.1.2 leaving the *durations* to the Schedule too, **both the SCOPE and the LENGTH of Acko's waiting periods are set per-policy and unbounded by the contract** — you cannot read the wording and know your worst case. **Ask: is the list fully enumerated in the wording, or can the insurer add conditions via the Schedule? An open list means two buyers of the same plan face different exclusions, and the insurer can add conditions at underwriting or renewal without a new UIN — treat it as a material weakening even where today's Schedule is clean.**
- **Mental-illness parity — check the ENABLING DEFINITIONS, not just the absence of an exclusion** *(new — Acko M2)*: the parity bullet below asks whether cover is *neutered by sub-limits/co-pay/waiting*. **Acko reveals a subtler failure mode — cover neutered by the absence of a qualifying FACILITY.** Its 57-page wording has **zero mentions of "Mental Illness" or "Mental Health Establishment"**: mental illness is neither covered explicitly nor excluded, but the **Hospital definition requires *"a fully equipped operation theatre"***, which most standalone psychiatric facilities lack — so a psychiatric admission may be **inadmissible despite nominal compliance** with the Oct-2022 mandate. SBI carries the full construct (defs 17/18/19); Care carries a Mental Illness definition. **Ask: does the wording define *Mental Illness*, deem a *Mental Health Establishment* to be a Hospital, and cover psychiatrists in the *Medical Practitioner* definition? Absent these, treat parity as unverified regardless of what the brochure claims.**
- **Third waiting tier — a separate longer list** *(new — Bajaj M2)*: some plans carve **joint replacement, vertebral/spine surgery, bariatric, Parkinson's, genetic/congenital-internal** into a **separate 36-month "first-3-years" list** that is *longer* than the 24-mo specific-disease list. Scoring only the 24-mo wait understates the real wait for these procedures — extract all three tiers (30-day / 24-mo / 36-mo), not just two
- **SI-gated waiting-reduction options** *(new — Aditya Birla MAX M2)*: if a plan sells paid PED/specific-disease waiting reductions, confirm the **SI band** they're offered in — some **vanish at high SI** (ABHI MAX: PED cut offered only ≤ ₹50L, specific-disease cut only ≤ ₹25L; a ₹1Cr buyer can't shorten any wait). This is a *third* gate beyond "does the reduction exist" and "who can opt it (channel vs individual)"
- **Enhancement-of-SI resets waiting afresh** on the increased portion — easy to miss
- **Co-pay** — any, and at what age? · **Sub-limits / disease capping** · Deductibles / aggregate deductible
- **Modern-treatment sub-limits** — the 12 IRDAI-mandated modern treatments (robotic surgery, oral chemo, deep-brain stimulation, etc.) are *covered* but often **capped** (e.g. 50% of SI or a fixed ₹). Extract each cap
- **Mental-illness coverage** — mandated since Oct 2022, but the *same* room caps / co-pay / sub-limits / waiting apply. Verify it's not neutered by sub-limits
- **Zone-based co-pay** — some plans impose co-pay if you're treated in a higher-cost city zone than you bought in (feeds M4 pricing too)
- **Rupee sub-limits frozen while the SI compounds** *(new — SBI M2)*: check whether the plan's **rupee-denominated sub-limits scale with the bonus-inflated SI, or stay fixed**. SBI states it outright (C.22.i / C.23.h): *"sub-limits… shall not increase proportionately with the Sum Insured"* — so as ECB grows a ₹25L policy to ₹75L, the ambulance stays ₹5,000 and the health check-up stays ₹5,000. Over a **40-year hold at 10–14% medical inflation every rupee cap decays to irrelevance**. This is the **slow-erosion mirror of M1's multiplier hard-cap check** (there the *bonus* is rupee-clipped; here the *benefits* are). **Ask: which benefits are rupee-capped rather than %-of-SI, and what are they worth in 20–40 years?**
- **Irreversible / inception-only option locks ("one-way doors")** *(new — SBI M2; also feeds M4)*: the existing *deductible-linked-discount* bullet treats discounts as a free trade — but the trade may be **permanent and asymmetric**. SBI: co-payment *"cannot be opted out of at subsequent Renewal"*; Aggregate Deductible *"cannot be opted out or reduced… however can be **increased**"*; ECB Safeguard and the Wellness/earn-back benefit are **inception-only** (can never be added later). **Ask, for every optional cover and discount lever: is it reversible, and is it inception-only?** A discount taken at 26 that can't be undone at 56 is a 30-year commitment. Buyer rule: **take the inception-only *benefits*, refuse the irreversible *discounts*.**
- **Permanent exclusions catalogue** — the full ~37-item list (obesity, cosmetic, refractive <7.5D, unproven, infertility, etc.)
- **Lifestyle-attributable illness exclusion** *(new — Care Supreme M2)*: distinct from the standard *"treatment of alcoholism/drug abuse"* item — check whether the wording permanently excludes illnesses **"attributable to" tobacco / alcohol / smoking** (i.e. the *downstream disease*, not just treatment of the addiction). The "attributable-to" construction (Care 4.2.24) reaches a smoker's COPD/lung cancer or a drinker's cirrhosis/pancreatitis and hands the insurer a **discretionary attribution-based denial** on some of the costliest common conditions. **Extract the exact wording and weight it — heavily for anyone who smokes or drinks; and note it as a candidate mechanism when a plan's ICR is low (M3).**
- **Underwriting-imposed permanent exclusion** *(new — HDFC M2)*: a plan can *accept* you but permanently carve out your declared PED (or apply a condition-specific loading/extra waiting) — check the counter-offer/permanent-exclusion clause, since an "accepted" proposal can still leave your main risk uncovered
- Non-disclosure consequences (feeds M6)

### Module 3 — Claims & Servicing *(IRDAI data + wording Claims section)*
- Incurred Claim Ratio (ICR) — latest + 3-yr trend · repudiation rate · complaint ratio & grievance disposal %
- **A low ICR is only a red flag when SERVICE data agrees — never screen on ICR alone** *(new — Acko/Royal Sundaram screening, Jul 2026)*: Stage 1's **hard binary "ICR 60–100%" filter (F3) produced a perverse result** and should not be applied standalone. **Care Supreme (ICR 64.53%) passed and became a finalist despite the study's WORST complaint ratio (42.00 per 10k claims); Acko (ICR 57.82%) was eliminated despite complaints of 15.58 — below industry, better than every finalist bar HDFC and Bajaj — a 95.75% CSR and 99.98% of claims paid within 3 months.** A 6.7-point ICR gap eliminated the plan with the *better* claims experience. ICR measures **rupees paid per rupee of premium**, so it is depressed by **cheap pricing and fast premium growth** (a ballooning denominator) as much as by stinginess — Acko's fall from 83.88% → 56.91% has exactly the growth signature. **Ask: does the low ICR come WITH poor service (complaints high, CSR low, slow settlement) — a genuine stinginess signal — or WITH good service — a pricing/growth signal?** **Always cross-check ICR against the complaint ratio, CSR and settlement speed before eliminating or flagging a plan on ICR.** *(Pairs with the peer-segment bullet below.)*
- **The BOOK'S AGE-MIX as an ICR confounder — a young digital book structurally under-claims** *(new — Acko M3)*: the framework now has two ICR safeguards — **benchmark against the correct peer segment** (Care M3) and **cross-check against service data** (Acko/Royal Sundaram screening). **Acko breaks both at once.** Its ICR is **~20 points below its own private-general peer group (57.82% vs 77.50%)** — so the peer correction fails — while **complaints sit below industry (15.58 vs 27.06) and 99.98% of claims settle within 3 months** — so the service cross-check says "not stingy". **When the two safeguards disagree, the missing variable is the AGE PROFILE of the insured book.** Claim frequency and severity rise steeply with age: an insurer selling digitally to **25–35-year-olds** shows a structurally lower ICR than a legacy insurer carrying decades of 50+ policyholders, **with no difference in generosity** — and it explains Acko's **27-point single-year ICR collapse**, which claims behaviour could not produce that fast. **Ask: what does the insurer's BOOK look like — age mix, vintage, growth rate? Where the peer benchmark and the service cross-check disagree, age-mix is the likely missing variable; if it isn't published, mark the ICR reading UNRESOLVED rather than forcing a verdict.** *(Confirming source: IRDAI age-band claims data / insurer portfolio disclosures.)*
- **Benchmark the ICR against the correct PEER SEGMENT, not the all-industry blend** *(new — Care Supreme M3)*: the framework said to pull the ICR but never said *what to compare it to* — and the blended "industry" figure is **dominated by PSU general insurers running ~99.84% ICR**, while **standalone health insurers (SAHIs) as a segment run ~68.06%** (IRDAI FY24-25). Judging a SAHI against the all-industry 84–87% therefore **overstates its shortfall by ~16 points**. Care Health at **64.53% looks catastrophic vs 84.38% but is only ~3.5 pts below its true SAHI peer group's 68.06%** — the same correction rescues Niva Bupa (61.22%); conversely, don't flatter a *general* insurer with the SAHI average. **Ask: what is this insurer's ICR relative to its OWN segment (SAHI / private general / PSU), and which way is it TRENDING?** A low-but-rising ICR (Care: 53.82→57.69→64.53) signals a **maturing, fast-growing book**, not tightening claims. **Never resolve a low-ICR flag on the blended average alone.**
- **Payout by count vs by amount** *(new — HDFC M3)*: CSR (% of claims settled *by count*) can be 97% while the insurer still disallows a chunk of each bill (R&C cuts, non-payables). Look for the **claim payout ratio by amount** / average deduction per settled claim — the real "how much of my bill did they actually pay"
- **Settlement-speed distribution (TAT buckets)** *(new — Aditya Birla MAX M3)*: pull the IRDAI claims-paid-by-ageing table — **% settled < 3 mo / 3–6 mo / 6–12 mo / > 1 yr** — a sharper "will they pay *quickly*" signal than CSR alone. A plan can post a high CSR yet have a slow tail; AB settles **100% within 3 months**. Report the distribution, not just the headline CSR
- **Cashless network** — size (per-district density, not raw count) + your city's top hospitals in-network
- **Metro-concentration ratio — city count ÷ national count** *(new — SBI M3)*: per-district density is still a **national average**, and it can hide the thing that matters. Take **one source** and compare each finalist's **absolute count in the buyer's city** *and* **that city's share of its national network**. SBI Super Health: **434 in Mumbai = 2.26%** of its 19,169 national vs **HDFC 520 = 3.60%** of 14,431 — SBI claims **33% more nationally** yet delivers **17% fewer** hospitals in the buyer's metro. A network can be **broad but thin** (SBI's plausibly tracks its rural bank-branch footprint). **Ask: what's the count in MY city, and what share of the national network is that?**
- **Network-count source dispersion as a data-quality red flag** *(new — SBI M3)*: before scoring network size, **check the spread across independent sources and whether the insurer publishes an official audited count at all**. SBI's published counts span **6,704 → 19,169 (2.8×)** and SBI **publishes no figure** — so the count is not a metric, and Stage-2's 5/5 network score resting on "~18,000" was unsound. **Treat a wide spread, or a refusal to publish, as a finding in itself — never as licence to pick the biggest number.**
- **Cashless-network discretion clause — the network is revocable** *(new — SBI M3)*: check whether the network is a **contractual schedule or a unilaterally revocable list**. SBI's wording: *"We reserve the right to modify, add or **restrict** any Network Provider for Cashless Facilities at **Our sole discretion**"*, plus an **Excluded Providers** blacklist (Code-Excl 11) that makes a delisted hospital's claim **wholly inadmissible** bar emergency stabilisation. **The hospital you bought the policy for can be removed mid-hold.** Over a 40-year horizon this matters more than the count. **Ask: revocable or scheduled? Is there a blacklist, and where is it published?**
- **Re-pull IRDAI data at module time — screening figures go stale** *(new — SBI M3)*: **company-wise ICR/CSR/complaints must be re-pulled at Module-3 time for every finalist.** Stage 2 ranked on **Bajaj 87% / SBI 82.2%**; FY24-25 IRDAI data says **Bajaj 74.59% / SBI 87.86%** — the two have **inverted**, and Bajaj's stated shortlist rationale (*"claims-reliability leader (ICR 87%)"*) **is no longer true**. **A stale ICR can invert the very rationale that shortlisted a plan** — where it does, say so and flag the affected plan's module and the decision tree.
- **Normalise the complaint ratio to a consistent denominator** *(new — Bajaj M3)*: aggregators quote complaints "per 10k" against different bases (per 10k **claims** vs per 10k **policies** vs per 10k **premium**). Before ranking insurers on complaints, confirm all figures use the **same denominator (prefer per-10k-claims)** — otherwise a "lower" number may just be a bigger base, not better service
- **Claim procedure mechanics** *(from wording)*: cashless pre-auth deadlines (planned 48h / emergency 24h); reimbursement windows (intimation, doc submission); **settlement TAT** (Optima: 15 days) and **penal interest** for delay (2% above bank rate)
- **Is the intimation deadline a CONDITION PRECEDENT? — the "notification trap"** *(new — Care Supreme M3)*: the bullet above extracts the deadlines as **timings**, but never asks the decisive legal question — **is missing one merely procedural, or is it drafted as a *condition precedent to liability* that can defeat an otherwise valid claim?** Care Supreme states it expressly: *"6.1.4(i) and 6.1.4(ii) are **precedent to admission of liability**"*, making the **48-hour intimation** and **30-day filing** windows claim-defeating in principle — so a fully covered ₹20L surgery can be resisted because the insured called on day 3. SBI, by contrast, carries an explicit condonation clause. **Ask, for every intimation/filing deadline: is it a condition precedent, and does any condonation / "reasons beyond control" relief exist?** A claim-defeating notification clause is a materially worse deal than the same deadline drafted as procedural. **Buyer rule: intimate within 48 hours of ANY admission, before you even know whether you'll claim.**
- **TPA vs in-house claims** — who adjudicates, and document burden
- **Real delivery on the 2024 IRDAI mandates** (see Regulatory Floor): does the insurer actually honour cashless-anywhere, 1-hr pre-auth, 3-hr discharge? Check recent policyholder reports / ombudsman awards, not the brochure
- Cashless-outside-India / 100%-cashless features

### Module 4 — Cost & Premium *(quote engine + wording discount/loading clauses)*
- Premium at current age, chosen SI · **age-loading curve** (45 / 55 / 65 / 75)
- **Claim impact on *premium*** — Favourable Claims Experience Discount: premium can *rise or fall* with your claim history (a softer, legal form of claim-linked pricing — distinct from banned claim-based loading)
- **Underwriting risk loadings** (Optima: up to 100%/diagnosis, 150%/person) — can apply at entry and renewal
- **SI-gated pre-policy medical-test cliff** *(new — SBI M4; also feeds M2)*: the framework scores *loadings* (M4) and *underwriting-imposed permanent exclusions* (M2) but never asks **at what SI/age the medical test that triggers them becomes mandatory** — which is the actual gate. **Extract the pre-policy-checkup grid.** SBI Super Health: **Platinum ₹10–25L → test only >45 yrs (a 26-yo escapes underwriting entirely); Platinum ₹30–50L → test from age 18; Platinum Infinite → test from 16.** A **₹5L step from ₹25L to ₹30L flips the buyer from zero scrutiny to full medicals + 150%-loading exposure (which persists through all renewals) + permanent-ICD-exclusion exposure.** The cliff is **invisible in the premium table**, so a per-lakh efficiency analysis (which makes the big SI look nearly free) **systematically understates the cost of the higher tier**. **Ask: where is the test line, does the target SI cross it, and who pays for the tests?** (SBI bears the full cost — a mitigant.) ⚠️ **Refinement (Care Supreme M2): credit the ABSENCE of a cliff as a positive, not merely score its presence.** Care Supreme requires **no tests up to age 65 at ANY SI** (₹5L→₹6Cr) with no PED declared — the mirror-image of SBI's ₹30L cliff. A flat/absent grid means a large SI can be bought **young with no loading or permanent-carve-out surface**; treat it as a quantifiable advantage for the "buy big early" strategy, the same way zone-agnostic pricing is credited.
- **Pre-announced escalation vs none — check BOTH sides** *(sharpened — SBI M4)*: the Bajaj bullet below says to extract a printed escalator "wherever present". **Equally, record its ABSENCE — it is a major, quantifiable advantage.** SBI's rate chart prints **no escalator** (only the standard §G.n IRDAI-approved revision power with 3 months' notice), while Bajaj contractually prints **+8%/yr**. Worked: at ₹10L, SBI starts **10% dearer** at 26 (₹19,446 vs ₹17,630) but is **~45% cheaper by 35** (₹19,446 flat vs Bajaj's ≈₹35,240 after 9 compounding years). **Over a 40-year hold the escalator dominates the entry-price gap — so never rank on entry premium alone.**
- **Deductible-linked discounts** (Optima: up to 65%) — the premium-vs-out-of-pocket trade-off
- Discount stack: family / loyalty / long-term / lifetime · instalment-premium option
- **Rate-chart transparency — can the premium curve be verified at all?** *(new — Care Supreme M4)*: every M4 tool below (age-loading curve, per-lakh SI-efficiency curve, escalator check, 10/20-yr outgo) **silently assumes the insurer publishes a rate chart**. Two finalists do, and it mattered: **SBI's** chart let M4 catch a **16–32% premium mis-read** carried through two modules, and **Bajaj's** chart is the only reason its **contractual +8%/yr escalator** was ever found. **Care Health publishes none** — pricing sits behind a quote engine requiring personal contact details. So you **cannot verify the age curve before buying**, **cannot detect an escalator's presence or absence from any document**, and **cannot audit a future renewal hike against a published baseline**. **Ask: does the insurer publish a public, verifiable rate chart? If not, treat every M4 figure as quote-dependent and unverifiable, and mark down the evidence quality in the score** — a plan whose price cannot be independently checked is structurally less auditable over a 40-year hold, however cheap today's quote is.
- **Is the pricing TABULAR or ALGORITHMIC? — the price you cannot audit** *(new — Acko M4; extends the rate-chart bullet above)*: Care M4 showed that an **unpublished** rate chart cannot be audited. **Acko exposes the sharper form: pricing that is *algorithmic*, so no rate table exists even in principle.** Acko generates each premium via an **"AI-based pricing engine"** from age, PIN code, SI, variant, medical history, family composition and underwriting outcome — its own aggregators state *"you will not find a single static ACKO premium chart."* Four consequences: **(1)** with a filed table you can point at the published rate for your age band to contest a renewal hike — **with an algorithm there is nothing to point at**; **(2)** a quote is **one personalised draw**, so like-for-like cross-plan comparison breaks down; **(3)** **two identical-risk buyers can legitimately pay different prices** on inputs neither can see; **(4)** the framework's own M4 toolkit (age-loading curve, per-lakh curve, 10/20-yr outgo) **assumes a stable published schedule and degrades without one**. **Ask: tabular or algorithmic? If algorithmic, mark every M4 figure as a single personalised draw, treat per-lakh comparisons as indicative only, and note the absent baseline for future re-pricing. Where pricing is algorithmic, a LONG-TERM (2–3 yr) premium lock becomes disproportionately valuable — it is the only way to fix the price at all.**
- **How do discounts COMBINE — additive, multiplicative, and is there an aggregate cap?** *(new — Care Supreme M4; refines the discount-stack bullet above)*: the stack bullet lists discounts but never asks how they compose. Care states it: *"All discounts… are **multiplicative** in nature, subject to **aggregate maximum discount… not exceeding 50% of the Premium**."* **Multiplicative stacking is worth materially less than additive** (three 15% discounts = **38.6%** off, not 45%), and the aggregate cap is the real ceiling. **Also classify each "discount": is it a genuine saving, or a RISK TRANSFER** (co-pay, deductible, network-restriction/gatekeeping modifiers) that you repay at claim time?
- **Wellness earn-back** — net premium after HealthReturns / activity rewards (can offset 5–100% in some plans)
- **GST on premium — now 0% for individual policies** *(updated — Aditya Birla MAX M4; was "18% GST", HDFC M4)*: the GST Council (56th meeting, 3 Sept 2025) **exempted all individual life & health insurance from GST effective 22 Sept 2025** — you now pay the **base premium only** (~15% real drop vs the old base × 1.18). ⚠️ **Group/employer cover is still 18%.** Historic quotes/older module files quoting "× 1.18" must be re-based to GST-free
- **Section 80D tax** — premium is deductible (₹25k self, ₹50k if senior parents); compute the *effective* post-tax cost. Note 80D applies under the **old tax regime** (nil/limited under the new regime — check the buyer's regime)
- **Zone-based pricing** — premium differs by city zone; note the zone you'd buy in and any zone-co-pay trap
- **Zone-agnostic pricing as a positive** *(new — SBI M4/M1)*: the framework previously treated zoning only as a *risk to detect*. Some products are explicitly **"Zone Agnostic"** (SBI Super Health, all five rungs) — one price nationwide **and no zone-co-pay clause**. For a **metro tier-1 buyer this is a real, quantifiable advantage**, not merely the absence of a defect: it removes both the Zone-A premium loading *and* the trap where a buyer priced in a cheaper zone is co-paid for treatment in a costlier one. **Credit it when present; don't just score its absence.**
- **Age-banded vs annual premium increase** *(new — Aditya Birla M4)*: does the premium step up only when you cross an age band (e.g. every 5 yrs — gentler, predictable) or rise every single year? Age-banded pricing is better for the policyholder — check which model the plan uses
- **Medical-inflation / block-repricing risk** — the real long-term threat: insurer repricing the *whole* product cohort upward. Check the product's revision history and frequency
- **Pre-announced / contractual annual base-rate escalation** *(new — Bajaj M4)*: some insurers now **print a fixed forward escalation directly in the rate chart** (Bajaj Health Guard: *"8% increase on base rates every year for policies with RID from 25 Oct 2026"*). This compounds *on top of* age-banding (8%/yr ≈ rate doubles every 9 yrs) and is a stronger, quantifiable signal than "check revision history." Extract it wherever present and weight it heavily — a low entry premium can be eroded fast
- **Premium-per-lakh SI-efficiency curve** *(new — Bajaj M4)*: report premium **per ₹1L of cover** across SI tiers, not just the headline premium. A large fixed base means each extra lakh of SI is cheaper (Bajaj @26: ₹1,763/lakh at ₹10L → ₹355/lakh at ₹1Cr). This reframes "how much cover to buy" — higher SI is disproportionately cheap, especially bought young
- Premium revision history · 10-yr and 20-yr projected total outgo · **base-vs-layered cost** (see Coverage Architecture)

### Module 5 — Insurer Quality *(IRDAI + financials)*
- Solvency ratio (min 1.5) + trend · years in business, promoter, financial strength/rating
- **Strip the sub-debt: core solvency, and the rating trigger is the real floor** *(new — SBI M5)*: the "min 1.5" above is misleading twice over. **(a)** A headline solvency can be **debt-flattered** — SBI General's **2.13× includes ₹700 Cr of sub-debt (borrowed capital, not equity); core solvency ex-sub-debt is 1.84×**. **(b)** IRDAI's 1.50 is **not the operative floor** — the **rating agency's negative trigger is** (ICRA's for SBI General: **1.70×**), because breaching it means a **downgrade**, which is what actually forces management action. On core solvency SBI sits **0.14 above its rating trigger**, not 0.63 above the regulatory one. **Always obtain core solvency excluding sub-debt and benchmark it against the agency's negative trigger.**
- **Decompose the combined ratio — is the loss CLAIMS-driven or EXPENSE-driven?** *(new — Acko M5)*: the framework asks *"is the loss in **your line**?"* (SBI) and *"how many **levers**?"* (Care), but never the prior question — **what is actually causing it?** A >100% combined ratio arises two ways with **opposite consequences for the policyholder**. **Claims-driven** (Care Supreme: claims +33% vs premium +21%) → the only fixes are **charge more or pay less**, both landing on you → **direct threat**. **Expense-driven** (Acko: **claims ratio a healthy 69.95%**, but **Expense of Management 47.71% of GDP**, pushing combined to 125.87%) → fixable by **scale and cost discipline, borne by shareholders** → **the policyholder is untouched**. **Ask: split the combined ratio into its claims and expense components before judging a loss-making insurer.** Weight a **125% combined ratio built on a 70% claims ratio** very differently from a **110% combined ratio built on a 95% claims ratio** — the first is a shareholder problem, the second is yours.
- **VC/PE-backed insurers — solvency sustained by FUNDING ROUNDS, not earnings** *(new — Acko M5)*: every ownership check here assumes a **corporate, bank or family promoter** (Bajaj/Allianz, SBI, Care/Burman). **A venture-backed insurtech is structurally different.** Acko has burned **~₹2,000 cr of paid-up capital** (₹2,796 cr paid-up vs ₹796 cr net worth; RoNW −24.28%), and its solvency **oscillates with funding events** — **1.91 → 1.68 → 2.75 → 1.89 → 2.30**, dipping to within **0.18 of the 1.50 floor** in FY22 and recovering on ₹300 cr (FY24) and ₹349 cr (FY25) equity issues. Distinct risks: **(a) funding-market dependency** — a capital freeze could strand solvency near the floor; **(b) exit pressure** — an IPO/PE exit creates pressure to show profit, which *could* mean repricing or claims tightening; **(c) no rated parent** to backstop. **Ask: plot solvency against equity issuance — does each recovery coincide with a funding round rather than profits? How many years of current losses does present capital fund? Is the insurer at operating break-even, and what does the sponsor's exit path imply for future pricing?**
- **Score the LICENSED ENTITY, not the brand or the group** *(new — Acko M5)*: press coverage of insurtechs and conglomerates routinely quotes **holdco** numbers. Acko's widely-reported *"₹424 cr FY25 loss"* belongs to **Acko Technology & Services**; the regulated insurer, **ACKO General Insurance Limited**, lost **₹193.38 cr** and posted its **first operating profit** — using the group figure would **overstate the insurer's loss by 2.2× and miss the inflection entirely**. **Ask: identify the exact licensed entity on the policy (IRDAI registration number) and take solvency, combined ratio, net worth and profitability from THAT entity's audited annual report.**
- **Attribute the combined ratio BY SEGMENT — whose losses are they?** *(new — SBI M5)*: the HDFC M5 bullet below treats a loss-making insurer as a uniform red flag — **too blunt for a multi-line insurer**. SBI General's **109.6% blended combined ratio is driven by MOTOR (126.4%→134.8%)**, while **PA and home are profitable** and the **health book shows an 87.86% ICR, 96% CSR and complaints down 38% — no distress in the line the buyer is actually purchasing**. **Ask: is the loss in *your* line, or elsewhere?** A motor-driven loss threatens a health policyholder only **indirectly** (group-level repricing pressure and capital consumption); a loss concentrated **in health** is a direct warning. A blended ratio cannot tell the two apart.
- **Retail-health share of the book — core customer or rounding error?** *(new — SBI M5)*: the "SAHI vs general insurer servicing focus" bullet below never **quantifies** it. Compute **retail health as a % of the insurer's GDPI**. SBI General: Health & PA = 35.2% of GDPI, but **group health is 64.3% of health GDPI → retail health ≈ 12.6% of the whole company**. **It cuts both ways:** a line that is a rounding error is more exposed to **repricing, deprioritisation and product withdrawal** (feeds M6) and attracts less product investment; but a **multi-line parent can cross-subsidise** health losses, which a pure-play SAHI (Care, Niva — retail health ≈ 100% of the book) cannot. **Score the trade-off; don't assume either structure is better.**
- **Monoline vs multi-line LOSS-ABSORPTION CAPACITY — count the levers** *(new — Care Supreme M5)*: the two bullets above are built for a **multi-line** insurer and **quietly break on a pure-play SAHI** — there is no segment attribution to perform (health *is* the company) and the "core customer vs rounding error" trade resolves trivially. Stopping there misses what matters: **how many ways can the insurer fix a deteriorating health book, and do any of them spare the policyholder?** A **multi-line** insurer has three exits — ① cross-subsidise from motor/fire/marine, ② reprice, ③ tighten claims. A **monoline SAHI has only ② and ③, and both land on the policyholder.** Care Health FY25 is the worked case: claims **+33%** vs premium **+21%**, operating profit **−86%**, solvency sliding **1.90→1.73→1.68**, with **100% of the deterioration in the line the buyer is purchasing** — and the two available levers were already visible as M3's worst-in-study complaint ratio (42/10k claims) and M4's §5.14 repricing power with **no published rate chart to audit it against**. **Ask: is this insurer monoline or multi-line, and weight a deteriorating combined ratio / falling solvency MORE heavily for a monoline showing identical numbers.** The SAHI "you are the core customer" positive is real but **cheap**; the missing cushion is the expensive half of the trade.
- **Health claims payout ratio** (Optima brochure cites 98%) — cross-check against IRDAI ICR
- **Profitability — RoE / combined ratio** *(new — HDFC M5)*: a chronically loss-making insurer tends to tighten claims scrutiny or push through steeper premium hikes; a profitable, growing insurer has less pressure to do either
- **Combined-ratio *trajectory*** *(new — Aditya Birla MAX M5)*: report the combined ratio's **level AND its 1–2 yr trend**, not just a snapshot. A young SAHI at 114% that has cut it from 121% in a year (backed by a deep-pocketed parent able to fund losses) is **de-risking** and far less worrying than one flat or worsening at the same level. Direction matters as much as the number
- Claims reputation & any regulatory action · SAHI vs general insurer servicing focus · TPA vs in-house
- **Ownership / foreign-JV-partner exit risk** *(new — Bajaj M5)*: check for recent or pending **changes in ownership** — especially a **foreign insurance parent exiting a JV** (e.g. Allianz's 2025 exit from Bajaj → now ~100% Bajaj Group, renamed "Bajaj General Insurance"). This removes global technical/reinsurance backing and adds brand/systems transition risk. **Key mitigant to verify: was the credit rating reaffirmed *after* the ownership change?** (Bajaj: AAA reaffirmed April 2026.) A wholly-domestic strong parent can be fine — but distinguish it from an insurer that retains a global parent (HDFC keeps Munich Re). ⚠️ **Extension (Care Supreme M5): also check whether any rating has been WITHDRAWN — when, and at whose request.** Care Health **passes** the reaffirmation test (India Ratings *upgraded* it to `IND AA-/Stable` on 29-Jan-2026, after the Burman takeover), but **CRISIL's `A+/Stable` was withdrawn in April 2024 at Care's own request**, mid-way through the contested takeover — removing an independent monitor exactly when scrutiny was most warranted. **A company-requested withdrawal is a soft negative signal even where another agency later rates the entity.** Also compare the rating *level* across finalists — `IND AA-` is two notches below the AAA carried by SBI General and Bajaj

### Module 6 — Fine Print & Longevity *(policy wording General Conditions)*
- **Moratorium period** (Optima: 60 months; note enhanced-SI resets the clock on the increase)
- Lifelong renewability · **entry max-age** · **grace period**
- **Extract the FULL list of non-renewal / mid-term-cancellation grounds, and test each for OBJECTIVITY** *(new — Acko M6)*: the framework tests *"lifelong renewability — must be YES"* as a **binary**, and never examines **the grounds on which renewal may still be refused**. Acko shows why that matters: **§5.1.14 lists three objective grounds** (fraud, non-disclosure, misrepresentation) but **§5.2.11 lists five — adding *"moral hazard"* and *"non-cooperation"*, neither defined anywhere in the 57-page wording** — and **§5.2.12(b) lets Acko terminate the policy MID-TERM for "non-cooperation"**, a power Care and SBI do not have. **A plan can therefore satisfy the veto test on paper while retaining a broad, undefined, unilateral discretion to exit the relationship.** **Ask: list every ground for (a) refusing renewal and (b) mid-term cancellation, and flag any undefined subjective standard ("moral hazard", "non-cooperation", "moral turpitude"). Where the wording gives two DIFFERENT lists in two clauses, treat the discrepancy as a live drafting defect and get the insurer's position in writing before buying.**
- **Does the wording impose ARBITRATION as a condition precedent to suit?** *(new — Acko M6)*: the framework maps the **Grievance → Ombudsman → IRDAI IGMS** ladder but never asks whether the wording adds a **costly extra hurdle**. Acko's **§5.1.12** sends quantum disputes to arbitration and makes an arbitral award *"a **condition precedent to any right of action or suit** upon the policy."* Arbitration costs an individual real money (arbitrator fees, representation) where the **Ombudsman is free**; and the clause is **asymmetric** — it does not apply where the insurer denies liability outright, i.e. it binds you precisely where the dispute is about **how much**, which is the likeliest dispute type with a low-ICR insurer. **No other finalist carries an arbitration clause.** **Ask: is arbitration mandatory, is an award a precondition to suing, and is the clause symmetric? Weigh against the statutory Ombudsman route (which cannot be contracted away) but treat mandatory arbitration as a real friction on redress.**
- **Claim-based loading** — banned on renewals, but confirm (distinct from M4's claims-experience discount)
- **Non-disclosure remedies** — the 3 options (permanent exclusion / added waiting / loading) + ab-initio cancellation risk
- **Condition Precedent to Admission of Liability** & Complete Discharge / Fraud clauses
- **ONGOING "material change" disclosure as a condition precedent — the relocation/occupation trap** *(new — Care Supreme M6)*: the framework tests disclosure thoroughly **at inception** (non-disclosure remedies; HDFC M2's underwriting carve-out; Care M3's intimation-condition-precedent) but **never asks whether the policyholder carries a CONTINUING duty of disclosure for the next forty years.** Care Supreme §5.18 imposes exactly that and makes it **claim-defeating**: *"It is a **condition precedent** to the Company's liability that the Policyholder shall **immediately notify** the Company in writing of any material change in the risk on account of change in **nature of occupation or business or current residing address**…"*, whereupon Care may *"adjust the scope of cover and/or the premium… **/reject the claim**"*. Reinforced by §5.20 **"No constructive Notice"** — telling an agent informally is worth nothing. **A 26-year-old will change job and city repeatedly before 66, and in a ZONE-PRICED product an address change is itself a pricing event** the insurer has a financial interest in enforcing. **Ask: is there a continuing duty to notify material changes (occupation / residence / health), and is it a condition precedent? Weight it higher for zone-priced products and long holds.** **Buyer rule: notify every address and occupation change in writing and keep the acknowledgement.**
- **Multiple policies / coordination of benefits** (matters if you also hold a top-up or corporate cover)
- Free-look mechanics · **cancellation & refund** (pro-rata vs *no refund once a claim is admitted*) · product revision/withdrawal + migration continuity
- **Grace-period coverage gap** *(new — Bajaj M6)*: don't assume "grace period" means you're still covered. Many plans **preserve continuity/credits** (waiting periods, NCB) if you renew within grace, **but do NOT pay claims that arise *during* the grace window** (Bajaj: no coverage during grace for annual payers; only instalment-mode gets grace-window cover). Confirm whether cover actually continues or only credits are protected — a claim in an unpaid grace window can be denied
- **Instalment-premium claim trap** *(new — HDFC M6)*: if you pay premium monthly/quarterly and then claim, the insurer can make **all remaining instalments of the year fall due at once and deduct them from your payout** — a hidden cost of "affordable EMI" that undercuts the M4 instalment benefit
- **Earn-back / wellness-wallet forfeiture on switching** *(new — Aditya Birla M6)*: plans with a rewards wallet (HealthReturns etc.) usually **forfeit the accrued balance if you port or cancel** — the more you value the earn-back, the more it **locks you in**, which matters if the insurer later hikes premiums. A plan with no wallet has no such lock-in
- **Wellness reward STRUCTURE: discount vs accrued wallet** *(new — SBI M6; refines the wallet-forfeiture bullet above)*: the two designs look identical in a brochure ("wellness rewards up to 30%") but have **opposite consequences for exit freedom**. Classify each plan's reward: **(a) accrued wallet/balance** (ABHI HealthReturns) → forfeited on exit → **lock-in, weigh against portability**; **(b) per-renewal premium discount** (SBI *Walk Healthy* — up to 30% off renewal, **no balance to forfeit**) → **no lock-in**. Same headline %, opposite effect. **A discount-structured reward is strictly better for a buyer who values the option to port** — so don't score the two as equivalent.
- **Renewability ≠ price guarantee (withdrawal-and-migrate re-pricing)** *(new — Aditya Birla MAX M6)*: "lifelong renewal" only guarantees **continuity**, not **premium**. Wordings typically renew *"provided the product is not withdrawn"* and, on withdrawal, force migration to a *similar* product — preserving SI/waiting/bonus credit but **NOT the rate**. Weight this risk **higher for young / still-loss-making products** (more likely to be withdrawn/replaced and re-priced); distinguish guaranteed renewability from guaranteed pricing when scoring longevity
- Grievance → Ombudsman → IRDAI IGMS escalation path

Each finalist moves through five phases. Phase 1 is new — it forces a wording read *before* feature scoring, so the modules are graded on the binding contract, not the brochure.

| Phase | Name | What happens | Source | Feeds |
|-------|------|--------------|--------|-------|

| **1** | Wording forensics | Read Section A (Definitions) + skim the clause map. Extract the **claim-lever definitions** (PED lookback, Any-One-Illness, R&C, Medically Necessary) and the section numbers for benefits/exclusions/conditions | Policy wording | M1, M2, M6 |
| **2** | Coverage & exclusions | Fill Modules 1 & 2 — what you get vs what they can deny. Verify every brochure claim against the wording | Wording | M1, M2 |

| **4** | Cost & insurer | Fill Modules 4 & 5 — real premium/age-curve, loadings, claims-experience discount; solvency & insurer strength | Quote engine + IRDAI | M4, M5 |

> ⚠️ **METHOD RULE — read rate charts and rating reports by RENDERING/PARSING the PDF, never by text scraping** *(new — SBI M4, extended SBI M5)*: insurer rate-chart PDFs stack several tables per page (**Single Child / Single Adult / 1A+1C / 1A+2C / 4A+3C floater**) with near-identical age-band rows. **Their text layer extracts captions out of visual order**, so a grep or text dump can silently attach the wrong caption to a table. This happened in SBI M1: the **1A+1C Family Floater** rows were read as **Single Adult**, overstating every premium by **16–32%** — carried for two modules until M4 rendered the pages as images and caught it.

Never quote a premium or a rating from a text dump.**
| **5** | Fine print & score | Fill Module 6, apply the override-veto check, then compute the weighted score | Wording | M6 + total |

> **Brochure vs wording rule:** the brochure sells; the wording binds. Phase 1 exists so that when Module 1 says "no room cap," it's because the *wording* says so — not the ad. Where the two conflict, the wording wins and the gap is itself a finding.

## Final Scoring Rubric

Score each finalist **1–5** per module, apply weights, sum for final rank.

| Module | Weight | Rationale |
|--------|:------:|-----------|
| Claims & servicing (M3) | 25% | A plan that won't pay is worthless regardless of features |
| Coverage & benefits (M1) | 25% | Defines what you're actually buying |
| Exclusions & waiting (M2) | 20% | Where most claim denials originate |
| Cost & premium (M4) | 15% | Matters, but lowest premium ≠ best value |
| Insurer quality (M5) | 10% | Must survive & stay fair for decades |
| Fine print & longevity (M6) | 5% | Deal-breakers here get an **override veto** on the total |

**Scale:** 1 = Poor, 2 = Below Average, 3 = Average, 4 = Good, 5 = Excellent

> **Override veto:** any Module 6 red flag (no lifelong renewal, claim-based loading, chronic product withdrawal) disqualifies a plan even with a high weighted score.
